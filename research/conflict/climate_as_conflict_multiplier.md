# Climate as a Conflict Multiplier

## Research Question

How can climate change increase the risk or severity of conflict without being treated as a simple, deterministic cause of war?

## Summary

Climate change does not mechanically produce war.

The IPCC assesses that climate hazards have affected armed conflict within countries, but that socioeconomic, political, and cultural factors are generally more influential. Climate is best understood as a risk multiplier that can worsen existing instability, inequality, exclusion, weak institutions, and resource dependence.

Project Anthropocene should therefore build conflicts from complete political causal chains rather than writing "drought caused war."

## Conflict Pathways

- crop losses increase food prices and fiscal stress;
- livelihood decline increases recruitment opportunities for armed groups;
- migration intensifies disputes over land, housing, services, and political membership;
- unequal adaptation creates grievances;
- disaster aid is captured or distributed along factional lines;
- water or grazing systems become militarized;
- state repression during emergency rule escalates unrest;
- and climate policy itself produces conflict through displacement, land acquisition, or energy-price shocks.

## Causal Chain

```text
Climate hazard
→ livelihood or revenue loss
→ unequal exposure and response
→ grievance, elite competition, or state repression
→ weakening institutions
→ armed mobilization becomes more attractive
→ conflict further destroys adaptive capacity
→ the next climate shock becomes more destabilizing
```

## Types of Conflict

### Local and Communal Conflict

Disputes over water points, grazing land, fisheries, urban housing, aid, or migration.

### Civil Conflict

Climate stress may worsen insurgency, separatism, coups, or state repression where institutions are already weak.

### Interstate Tension

Shared rivers, food exports, migration routes, strategic minerals, and infrastructure can intensify rivalry. Direct interstate war still requires political decisions and strategic calculations.

### Organized Crime and Warlordism

Where formal authority retreats, armed actors may control water, food, transport, energy, border crossings, or salvage.

## Conflict Dampeners

Climate pressure can also increase cooperation.

Risk is reduced by:

- trusted institutions,
- inclusive resource-sharing agreements,
- early warning,
- social safety nets,
- legitimate local governance,
- diversified livelihoods,
- and credible dispute resolution.

These factors should explain why similarly exposed regions experience different outcomes.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Water treaties and grain agreements can be as important as military alliances.
- Security forces become infrastructure protectors and disaster responders.
- Armed groups may gain legitimacy by providing services.
- Climate-adaptation projects create new territories worth contesting.
- Conflict and peace vary sharply between neighboring regions exposed to similar hazards.
- The most durable powers combine coercive capacity with reliable service delivery.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- "Climate wars" without political actors and decisions.
- Treating scarcity as automatically violent.
- Assuming migration inherently causes conflict.
- Ignoring cooperation and adaptation.
- Attributing a real historical conflict to climate alone.
- Escalating every resource dispute into nuclear war.

## Proposed Canon

Climate change contributes to the century of fragmentation by amplifying existing political fractures. No major war should enter canon without a documented chain involving institutions, interests, identities, resources, and strategic decisions.
