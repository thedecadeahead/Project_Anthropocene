# Initial Research Questions

This document is the starting research queue for Project Anthropocene.

Questions are prioritized by how strongly they affect the rest of the setting. Work should generally begin with physical and biological constraints, then move into social responses and fictional canon.

## Priority Levels

- **P0 — Foundational:** The setting cannot be responsibly built without answering this.
- **P1 — Structural:** Determines major regional, political, technological, or demographic outcomes.
- **P2 — Enrichment:** Adds depth and specificity after the basic world is stable.
- **P3 — Local:** Best answered within a regional sourcebook, story, or campaign.

## Phase One: Planetary Baseline

### P0 — Climate Scenario

1. Which emissions and socioeconomic pathway best represents a world where mitigation remains inadequate through the mid-21st century?
2. What global mean warming range is plausible by 2050, 2100, 2150, and 2200 under that pathway?
3. How much warming continues after emissions decline because of Earth-system inertia?
4. Which outcomes are robust across models, and which depend heavily on uncertain feedbacks?
5. What assumptions would be required for the setting to reach a severe but scientifically defensible climate state?

### P0 — Regional Climate

1. Which regions experience the greatest increase in dangerous heat and humidity?
2. Where do drought, rainfall variability, flooding, wildfire, and storm risk change most significantly?
3. Which currently populated regions become seasonally dangerous without mechanical cooling?
4. Which northern or high-altitude regions become more agriculturally or strategically important?
5. How much local detail can climate models credibly support beyond 2100?

### P0 — Sea Level and Coastlines

1. What sea-level range is plausible by 2100, 2150, 2200, and 2250?
2. How should low-probability ice-sheet instability be represented without treating it as certain?
3. Which major cities, ports, deltas, islands, military facilities, and industrial zones face chronic inundation or abandonment?
4. Which places can plausibly defend themselves through barriers, elevation, drainage, or managed retreat?
5. What infrastructure fails before land is permanently submerged?

## Phase Two: Human Carrying Systems

### P0 — Agriculture and Food

1. At what temperatures do major staple crops suffer during germination, flowering, pollination, and grain filling?
2. How do heat, drought, humidity, pests, soil degradation, and extreme weather interact to affect yields?
3. Which breadbaskets decline, shift, or become unreliable?
4. What adaptations are plausible: altered planting dates, irrigation, gene editing, protected agriculture, new crops, or relocation?
5. What simultaneous multi-breadbasket failures are plausible?
6. How do fisheries, livestock, fertilizer, refrigeration, transport, and storage affect food security?
7. Under what conditions does food scarcity become chronic rather than episodic?

### P0 — Fresh Water

1. Which aquifers and river basins are already being depleted or overallocated?
2. How do glacier loss, snowpack change, drought, saltwater intrusion, and rainfall variability alter supply?
3. Where is desalination practical, and what energy, infrastructure, and waste constraints remain?
4. Which cities and agricultural regions become untenable because of water rather than temperature?
5. What governance arrangements reduce or intensify interstate and internal water conflict?

### P0 — Habitability and Health

1. What combinations of heat and humidity exceed safe limits for work, sleep, pregnancy, childhood, and old age?
2. How effective are cooling, urban design, work-hour changes, shelters, and personal protective systems?
3. Which diseases expand, contract, or shift geographically?
4. How do malnutrition, air pollution, smoke, displacement, and health-system disruption interact?
5. What separates a lethal heat event from a mass-casualty infrastructure failure?

### P1 — Ecology

1. Which ecosystems are likely to transform rather than simply move?
2. Which keystone species, pollinators, forests, reefs, wetlands, and fisheries face the highest risk?
3. Where does rewilding follow human retreat?
4. How do invasive species and assisted migration reshape regional ecologies?
5. What ecological services become scarce enough to influence politics and trade?

## Phase Three: Cascading Failure

### P0 — Global Trade and Supply Chains

1. Which commodities and components have the most geographically concentrated production?
2. How do repeated port closures, crop failures, fuel disruptions, export restrictions, and conflict propagate through trade networks?
3. Which sectors fail quickly without international supply chains?
4. Which sectors can be regionalized or substituted?
5. What minimum global coordination is required to maintain satellites, semiconductors, medicines, aviation, and complex energy systems?

### P1 — Insurance, Finance, and Property

1. When and where do private insurance markets withdraw from climate risk?
2. How do mortgage markets, municipal bonds, pensions, sovereign debt, and property taxation respond?
3. What happens when governments become insurers of last resort?
4. How does repeated disaster spending reduce maintenance and long-term investment?
5. Can financial crises accelerate managed retreat, state failure, or authoritarian governance?

### P1 — Infrastructure

1. How do heat, flooding, wildfire, storms, saltwater, and maintenance backlogs affect grids, roads, rail, ports, water systems, and communications?
2. Which infrastructure can operate autonomously or degrade gracefully?
3. What systems fail because replacement parts or expertise disappear rather than direct physical damage?
4. Which legacy systems remain usable for a century or more with maintenance?
5. What infrastructure becomes the nucleus of surviving city-states and regional powers?

### P1 — Pandemics and Biosphere Change

1. How does climate-driven range expansion alter zoonotic and vector-borne disease risk?
2. How does displacement affect surveillance, vaccination, sanitation, and treatment?
3. Which technological improvements reduce pandemic risk despite institutional decline?
4. What plausible pandemic could materially weaken globalization without becoming an implausible extinction event?

## Phase Four: Technology Before and During the Break

### P0 — Technology Baseline

1. How far might artificial intelligence, robotics, biotechnology, medicine, energy, materials, and manufacturing advance by 2050–2100?
2. Which technologies are physically plausible but economically or institutionally uncertain?
3. Which technologies improve resilience but deepen inequality or political control?
4. Which systems require globally distributed supply chains?
5. Which advanced technologies could be locally maintained after globalization fractures?

### P1 — Energy

1. What mix of solar, wind, storage, nuclear fission, geothermal, hydroelectricity, synthetic fuels, and possibly fusion is plausible?
2. What grid technologies make regions more resilient or more dependent?
3. Which energy systems can be maintained under declining industrial capacity?
4. How do critical minerals constrain deployment and repair?
5. Does abundant electricity solve local adaptation while leaving food, ecology, governance, and materials unresolved?

### P1 — Artificial Intelligence and Robotics

1. Which tasks are likely to be automated before major institutional breakdown?
2. What hardware, energy, data, networking, and maintenance does advanced AI require?
3. Could smaller local models remain useful when global cloud infrastructure fragments?
4. Which robots plausibly maintain farms, warehouses, power systems, transportation, and hazardous environments?
5. How would autonomous systems behave when owners, networks, or institutions disappear?

### P1 — Biotechnology and Agriculture

1. How much heat, drought, salinity, disease, and nutritional stress can crop engineering plausibly mitigate?
2. What biological constraints cannot be engineered away cheaply?
3. How dependent are engineered crops on seed production, intellectual property, fertilizer, irrigation, and controlled environments?
4. What new risks arise from ecological release, monoculture, or centralized control?

## Phase Five: Social and Political Transformation

### P1 — Migration and Population

1. How many people may be displaced internally and internationally under different scenarios?
2. Which estimates refer to exposure, displacement, migration, or permanent relocation?
3. Where are the major origin, transit, and destination regions?
4. What happens when destination regions also face housing, food, water, and political stress?
5. How might fertility, mortality, urbanization, and migration reshape global population by 2200?

### P1 — State Capacity and Legitimacy

1. What causes modern states to lose capacity gradually rather than collapse instantly?
2. Which functions are most likely to decentralize first?
3. When do cities, provinces, militaries, utilities, corporations, or mutual-aid networks replace national institutions?
4. Which political systems adapt best to repeated emergencies?
5. How do emergency powers become permanent?

### P1 — Conflict

1. What does research support about climate as a threat multiplier rather than a singular cause of war?
2. Which resource conflicts are plausible, and which popular narratives are overstated?
3. How do food shocks, migration, inequality, ethnic politics, and state repression interact?
4. How do nuclear deterrence and advanced weapons shape wars during global fragmentation?
5. What prevents conflict from escalating into total human extinction?

### P1 — Fragmentation of Globalism

1. What sequence of trade restrictions, alliance failures, debt crises, communications breakdowns, wars, and domestic pressures could end the First Global Age?
2. Which international systems persist longest?
3. Does the internet fragment politically before it fails physically?
4. Which regional blocs retain trade, standards, and scientific exchange?
5. What is the threshold between reduced globalization and true systemic fragmentation?

## Phase Six: Recovery and the Setting Present

### P1 — Chronology of the Collapse

1. Is the collapse best represented as a century-long transition rather than a named single event?
2. What period do later historians identify as the decisive break?
3. Which crises are globally shared, and which are remembered only regionally?
4. What records survive, and who controls historical interpretation?
5. When does population and institutional decline stabilize?

### P1 — Regional Survivors

1. Which regions retain dense populations, advanced industry, or reliable agriculture?
2. Which become migration destinations and later powers?
3. Which are abandoned but remain valuable for salvage, biodiversity, energy, or archaeology?
4. Which communities preserve technical knowledge and manufacturing capacity?
5. How connected is the world by the setting year?

### P1 — Setting Date

1. What exact year best balances recognizable ruins, inherited advanced technology, ecological transformation, and new political cultures?
2. How many generations separate player characters from the decisive collapse?
3. What old-world infrastructure remains physically credible at that date?
4. How mythologized is the 21st century?
5. Should the principal setting year be approximately 2225, 2250, or another date?

### P2 — Daily Life

1. What do people eat, wear, build, repair, celebrate, fear, and trade?
2. What is ordinary transportation?
3. What education and medicine are available at different settlement tiers?
4. How do people experience heat, seasons, wilderness, ruins, and advanced machinery?
5. What does a child born in this world consider normal?

### P2 — Languages and Cultures

1. Which languages spread, merge, fragment, or become ceremonial?
2. How do migration and network isolation alter identity?
3. What religions, philosophies, festivals, and taboos emerge from the Transition Era?
4. How do communities remember those blamed for the collapse?
5. Which old-world media survive as cultural touchstones?

## First Research Sequence

The recommended first sequence is:

1. Select the climate and socioeconomic scenario.
2. Establish warming and sea-level ranges through 2250.
3. Research staple-crop heat limits and multi-breadbasket risk.
4. Map habitability, water, and migration pressures.
5. Define the plausible technology baseline before fragmentation.
6. Model cascading impacts on trade, finance, and state capacity.
7. Draft the first provisional master timeline.
8. Select the principal setting year.

## Completion Standard

A question is not considered answered merely because a source was found.

Before closure, the associated research should include:

- at least one strong primary or assessment source,
- corroboration where the claim is consequential,
- scenario and geographic context,
- uncertainty and counterevidence,
- a clearly stated causal chain,
- and specific implications for canon.
