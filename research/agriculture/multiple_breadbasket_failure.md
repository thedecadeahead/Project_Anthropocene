# Multiple Breadbasket Failure

## Research Question

How could simultaneous crop losses in several major producing regions create a global systemic crisis?

## Summary

The danger is not that one crop fails everywhere. It is that multiple exporting regions experience correlated heat, drought, flood, or pest shocks during the same season while governments and markets react defensively.

Global food systems are vulnerable because production, export capacity, fertilizer, shipping, finance, and grain trading are geographically concentrated.

## Core Mechanisms

- Concurrent extreme heat during reproductive stages.
- Linked drought and heat across distant regions.
- Flooding or excess rain that prevents planting or harvest.
- Export restrictions imposed after domestic shortages.
- Fertilizer and fuel disruptions that reduce the following season's output.
- Financial speculation and panic purchasing.
- Humanitarian systems competing for the same limited grain.

Research also indicates that some climate and crop models may underestimate synchronized low-yield risk because they do not fully reproduce certain large-scale atmospheric patterns.

## Causal Chain

```text
Concurrent low yields in major exporters
→ futures prices and shipping demand spike
→ exporting states restrict sales
→ import-dependent states lose access
→ food subsidies strain budgets
→ unrest and political repression increase
→ fertilizer, credit, and fuel access decline
→ the next planting season is weakened
→ an acute shock becomes a multi-year crisis
```

## Worldbuilding Implications

A plausible first global food emergency should include:

- uneven regional harvest loss rather than universal crop death;
- grain-export restrictions;
- rationing and subsidy crises;
- substitution among wheat, rice, maize, millet, sorghum, roots, and synthetic foods;
- black markets and strategic grain reserves;
- and lasting mistrust of global commodity markets.

Repeated events could motivate later city-states and regional blocs to treat food independence as a security doctrine.

## Severe but Plausible Interpretation

By the second half of the 21st century, a major synchronized harvest shock may no longer be treated as a rare anomaly. Even where total global calories remain theoretically sufficient, trade breakdown and unequal purchasing power can produce famine in import-dependent regions.

The most damaging event is likely a compound crisis involving weather, war, shipping disruption, and export controls—not climate alone.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- One heat wave killing all major crops worldwide.
- Treating global food quantity as identical to local food access.
- Ignoring reserves, substitution, adaptation, and trade.
- Assuming high-latitude expansion fully offsets losses.
- Using an exact probability without matching the source's scenario and definitions.

## Proposed Canon

Project Anthropocene should include at least one historically decisive multi-breadbasket crisis between 2050 and 2100, but its date and participants should remain provisional until regional crop, trade, and geopolitical research is complete.
