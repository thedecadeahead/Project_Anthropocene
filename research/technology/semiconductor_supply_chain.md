# Semiconductor Supply Chains and Technological Survival

## Research Question

What happens to advanced electronics when globalization fractures, and which kinds of chips remain manufacturable?

## Summary

Semiconductors are among the clearest limits on technological continuity.

The industry is not one factory or one country. It is a globally distributed system involving design software, intellectual property, lithography tools, specialty chemicals, ultrapure materials, wafers, fabrication, packaging, testing, precision optics, and highly trained labor.

Project Anthropocene should not assume that a surviving city can simply restart advanced chip production from blueprints.

## Technology Tiers

### Tier 1 — Leading-Edge Logic

Used for frontier artificial intelligence, high-performance computing, and premium consumer devices. Requires the most complex and globally concentrated supply chains.

### Tier 2 — Advanced Mainstream Logic and Memory

Supports servers, vehicles, industrial control, communications, and sophisticated robotics. Still highly demanding but somewhat more geographically distributed.

### Tier 3 — Mature-Node Industrial Chips

Microcontrollers, sensors, power-management chips, analog devices, and communications components. These are less glamorous but more important to keeping infrastructure running.

### Tier 4 — Discrete and Simple Electronics

Power transistors, diodes, basic controllers, and rugged legacy components. Most plausible foundation for regional post-fragmentation manufacturing.

## Survival Mechanisms

- stockpiling packaged chips and spare boards;
- recovering components from obsolete equipment;
- maintaining mature-node fabrication plants;
- redesigning systems around available components;
- using field-programmable hardware;
- standardizing rugged open architectures;
- preserving design files, process recipes, and test equipment;
- and concentrating advanced production inside secure industrial blocs.

## Causal Chain

```text
Trade conflict and transport disruption
→ shortages of tools, chemicals, wafers, and spare parts
→ leading-edge fabrication becomes geographically isolated
→ product designers qualify older substitute components
→ repair and salvage markets expand
→ complex consumer electronics become scarce
→ infrastructure shifts toward standardized mature-node hardware
→ technological regions diverge by semiconductor access
```

## Worldbuilding Implications

- A city may possess advanced software but lack replacement hardware.
- Century-old industrial controllers remain in use because their architecture is reproducible.
- Chips and functioning circuit boards become strategic salvage.
- Electronics are designed for modular replacement, repair, and long service life.
- The most advanced polities guard fabs, chemical plants, and lithography equipment as national assets.
- Other regions maintain sophisticated machinery with a finite inherited stock of irreplaceable components.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- All electronics failing immediately after trade disruption.
- Isolated settlements manufacturing frontier processors from raw sand.
- Assuming advanced chips are always necessary; many essential systems can use mature nodes.
- Ignoring packaging, testing, specialty chemicals, software tools, and precision maintenance.
- Treating semiconductor knowledge as equivalent to semiconductor capacity.

## Proposed Canon

Leading-edge chip production survives only within a few highly integrated industrial powers. Mature-node electronics remain much more widespread, while salvage, standardization, and hardware longevity become central features of daily technology.
