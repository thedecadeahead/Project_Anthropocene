# Population Change After Crisis

## Research Question

How might fertility, mortality, aging, migration, and institutional disruption reshape the global population by the setting era?

## Summary

Project Anthropocene should not assume that climate change directly kills most of humanity.

The more plausible demographic transformation is uneven and cumulative:

- fertility continues falling in many regions;
- populations age before institutions are ready;
- migration redistributes people toward safer and more productive regions;
- mortality rises during wars, heat disasters, famine, epidemics, and health-system collapse;
- and some regions lose population through sustained out-migration rather than mass death.

The United Nations World Population Prospects 2024 medium projection places the global population peak near 10.3 billion in the mid-2080s, followed by a slight decline to roughly 10.2 billion by 2100. Project Anthropocene diverges from that baseline only when its fictional crises materially alter fertility, mortality, or migration.

## Demographic Drivers

### Fertility

Fertility often declines with urbanization, education, delayed family formation, insecurity, high housing costs, and improved access to contraception. Crisis can suppress births temporarily, though outcomes vary by region and policy.

### Mortality

Climate-related mortality is mediated through heat, disease, food insecurity, conflict, pollution, disasters, and health-system failure. Most losses are likely to be regionally concentrated rather than globally uniform.

### Aging

Low fertility and longer lifespans create aging societies. When infrastructure and pensions weaken, care work becomes a major economic and political burden.

### Migration

Migration can depopulate origin regions while rejuvenating destination labor forces. It can also create sharp sex, age, and skill imbalances.

## Causal Chain

```text
Low fertility and aging
→ smaller working-age population
→ rising care and fiscal burdens
→ automation and migration become more valuable
→ political conflict over membership and redistribution
→ repeated crises increase mortality and suppress births
→ some states enter rapid demographic contraction
```

## Working Population Principle

No global population figure beyond 2100 should become canon yet.

The setting should instead use regional demographic categories:

- growing destination regions,
- aging maintenance states,
- depopulating hazard zones,
- youthful but stressed regions,
- mobile corridor populations,
- and low-density recovery territories.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Some powerful states actively recruit migrants to preserve industry and care systems.
- Abandoned suburbs and towns may result from aging and out-migration before physical destruction.
- Automation becomes a demographic adaptation as much as a technological luxury.
- Children may be rare and culturally protected in some settlements.
- Other regions retain younger populations but lack capital and stable infrastructure.
- Population records become unreliable after statistical systems fragment.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Choosing an enormous death toll solely to make the world feel post-apocalyptic.
- Assuming disaster always raises fertility or always lowers it.
- Treating all countries as moving through demographic transition at the same rate.
- Ignoring aging, dependency ratios, and care labor.
- Presenting exact 22nd-century population numbers without modeled assumptions.

## Proposed Canon

The old global population peak occurs before or during the century of fragmentation. The subsequent decline is driven by low fertility, aging, migration, and elevated regional mortality rather than a single extinction-scale event.
