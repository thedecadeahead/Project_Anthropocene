# Baseline Climate and Socioeconomic Scenario

## Research Question

Which emissions and socioeconomic pathway best supports Project Anthropocene's premise: inadequate climate mitigation, continued technological progress, escalating climate damage, and eventual fragmentation of the global order?

## Decision

Project Anthropocene will not treat any single Shared Socioeconomic Pathway as a literal prediction.

The working baseline is a **hybrid pathway**:

1. **2025-2050:** broadly consistent with present-day policy trajectories and incomplete implementation of national climate commitments.
2. **2050-2100:** increasing geopolitical rivalry, weakened international coordination, uneven development, and adaptation stress make the world progressively more similar to the social logic of **SSP3-7.0**.
3. **After 2100:** emissions decline unevenly because of resource limits, technological substitution, population change, state fragmentation, and the contraction of global industry—not because humanity successfully coordinates a clean transition.

This is a fictional scenario informed by real models. It is not itself an official modeled pathway.

## Why Not SSP5-8.5?

SSP5-8.5 remains scientifically useful as a high-emissions stress test, but it assumes very high fossil-fuel use and rapid energy-intensive development across much of the century.

Project Anthropocene instead requires:

- worsening climate risk,
- substantial technological progress,
- growing geopolitical fragmentation,
- uneven economic development,
- declining global coordination,
- and eventual disruption of the industrial systems needed to sustain the highest emissions trajectory.

That combination resembles SSP3-7.0 more closely than SSP5-8.5 after mid-century.

SSP5-8.5 should therefore be used as an **upper-bound sensitivity case**, not the canonical baseline.

## Why Not Simply Use Current Policies?

The United Nations Environment Programme's 2025 Emissions Gap Report estimates approximately **2.8°C of warming over this century under current policies**.

That estimate is a snapshot of policies and assumptions as they existed in 2025. It does not model the specific later political breakdown, trade fragmentation, food crises, conflicts, and institutional decay imagined by Project Anthropocene.

The setting therefore begins close to a current-policies trajectory, then diverges as its fictional history develops.

## Physical Climate Anchors

The baseline should remain consistent with the following assessed findings:

- Near-term warming differs only modestly among major scenarios.
- Around mid-century, warming near 2°C above 1850-1900 is plausible across intermediate and high pathways.
- By 2081-2100, the IPCC central estimate is approximately 2.7°C under SSP2-4.5 and 3.6°C under SSP3-7.0.
- Beyond 2100, warming depends strongly on the emissions pathway and becomes increasingly uncertain.
- Under the official extended SSP3-7.0 scenario, median warming at 2300 reaches 8.2°C, but this assumes the SSP extension rather than Project Anthropocene's post-collapse emissions decline.

## Canonical Planning Envelope

For worldbuilding, the project will initially use this global mean surface warming envelope relative to 1850-1900:

| Period | Working central value | Working range | Classification |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 2021-2040 | 1.5°C | 1.2-1.8°C | IPCC assessed range across relevant scenarios |
| 2041-2060 | 2.0°C | 1.6-2.6°C | IPCC SSP2-4.5 to SSP3-7.0 range |
| 2081-2100 | 3.2°C | 2.5-4.0°C | Project inference between current policies and SSP3-7.0 |
| 2140-2160 | 3.8°C | 3.0-5.0°C | Project inference; low confidence |
| 2190-2210 | 4.1°C | 3.1-5.5°C | Project inference; low confidence |
| 2240-2260 | 4.2°C | 3.0-5.8°C | Project inference; very low confidence |

These post-2100 values are **not quoted projections from the IPCC**. They are planning assumptions chosen to represent continued warming after a severe 21st century followed by declining but non-zero emissions.

They must be revised when the project's emissions, population, energy, and industrial histories become more detailed.

## Causal Logic

```text
Insufficient mitigation through mid-century
→ warming approaches or exceeds 2°C
→ adaptation costs and regional losses accelerate
→ food, water, migration, insurance, and infrastructure pressures intensify
→ states prioritize domestic security and strategic supply chains
→ global cooperation weakens
→ emissions remain high longer than current-policy optimism assumes
→ climate damages and geopolitical fragmentation undermine industrial growth
→ emissions eventually decline through contraction and technological substitution
→ atmospheric concentrations remain elevated
→ warming plateaus only gradually while sea level continues rising
```

## Canon Implications

This baseline supports a world in which:

- the climate crisis is severe enough to contribute materially to civilizational fragmentation;
- collapse remains a social and institutional process rather than a physically instantaneous event;
- some advanced technologies are developed before the global break;
- the world avoids the most extreme 2300 SSP5-8.5 outcome;
- different regions experience radically different levels of habitability and state continuity;
- and the setting can remain recognizably Earth-like while profoundly transformed.

## Uncertainty

The largest unresolved uncertainties are:

- actual global emissions after 2050,
- climate sensitivity,
- carbon-cycle feedbacks,
- methane and aerosol trajectories,
- the pace of industrial contraction,
- the scale of carbon dioxide removal,
- future population,
- and whether large-scale technological systems survive long enough to reduce emissions deliberately.

## Review Triggers

Revise this baseline when:

- a newer IPCC assessment supersedes AR6,
- the project completes its energy and technology baseline,
- a detailed emissions history is written,
- population and industrial-output assumptions are established,
- or regional climate research exposes contradictions with the global envelope.
