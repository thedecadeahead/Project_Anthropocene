# Sea-Level Rise Planning Bounds

## Research Question

What sea-level rise range is plausible by 2100, 2150, 2200, and 2250 for Project Anthropocene's severe but non-maximal climate pathway?

## Summary

Sea level is a lagging consequence of warming. Even if global temperature later stabilizes, ocean thermal expansion and land-ice loss continue for centuries.

For Project Anthropocene, sea-level rise should not be represented as a single uniform number. Global mean rise, local relative rise, storm surge, subsidence, erosion, groundwater loss, and saltwater intrusion combine differently by region.

## Assessed Foundation

The IPCC Sixth Assessment Report provides scenario-based global mean sea-level projections through 2100 and indicative longer-term ranges. NOAA's 2022 technical report provides updated United States regional scenarios and emphasizes that relative sea level along much of the U.S. coast is expected to rise rapidly through 2050.

A key worldbuilding point is that coastal systems fail before permanent inundation reaches their final extent. Repeated flooding, insurance withdrawal, drainage failure, saltwater intrusion, port disruption, and declining property values can force retreat decades before a location is continuously underwater.

## Working Global Planning Envelope

The following values are Project Anthropocene planning assumptions relative to approximately year 2000 levels. They combine assessed projections with cautious post-2100 extrapolation.

| Date | Working central value | Working range | Confidence |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| 2050 | 0.25 m | 0.18-0.35 m | medium-high |
| 2100 | 0.75 m | 0.50-1.10 m | medium |
| 2150 | 1.25 m | 0.75-2.00 m | low-medium |
| 2200 | 1.75 m | 1.00-3.00 m | low |
| 2250 | 2.20 m | 1.20-4.00 m | very low |

These are not direct quotations from a single model. They are setting bounds chosen for consistency with a severe pathway in which warming exceeds 3°C and ice loss continues, while avoiding automatic adoption of the highest low-likelihood outcomes.

## High-End Sensitivity Case

A separate non-canonical sensitivity case should test substantially faster ice-sheet loss.

This case may be used to evaluate:

- abandonment of major deltas,
- loss of low islands,
- failure of coastal megacities,
- relocation of ports and naval facilities,
- and long-term coastline change.

It should not become canon without a clearly stated ice-sheet mechanism and evidence review.

## Local Modifiers

Local relative sea level may differ greatly from the global mean because of:

- land subsidence,
- groundwater and hydrocarbon extraction,
- sediment compaction,
- glacial isostatic adjustment,
- tectonic movement,
- ocean circulation,
- and regional gravitational effects from ice loss.

Deltas and heavily pumped coastal basins can experience effective rise much faster than the global average.

## Infrastructure Failure Sequence

```text
Higher mean sea level
→ more frequent high-tide flooding
→ storm surge reaches farther inland
→ drainage and wastewater systems fail more often
→ insurance and credit retreat
→ maintenance and tax bases shrink
→ protective infrastructure becomes harder to finance
→ managed or unmanaged retreat accelerates
→ permanent inundation arrives after social abandonment
```

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Many coastal districts are abandoned before they are fully submerged.
- Wealthy or strategic enclaves may remain behind barriers long after surrounding regions retreat.
- Ports migrate inland or upriver where geography permits.
- Deltas become zones of unstable channels, saline agriculture, mobile settlements, and contested engineering.
- Ruined coastal infrastructure remains accessible by boat, diving equipment, or during low water.
- New shorelines continue moving during the setting era; maps are political and temporary.
- The Great Lakes and other inland transport systems gain strategic value without becoming direct substitutes for ocean trade.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Treating all coasts as rising at the same rate.
- Assuming every threatened city is simply submerged at once.
- Using 10-30 feet of rise by 2100 as the baseline.
- Ignoring storm surge, subsidence, erosion, and infrastructure economics.
- Freezing the coastline permanently in the setting year.

## Proposed Canon

Use approximately 0.75 meters of global mean sea-level rise by 2100 and approximately 2.2 meters by 2250 as initial mapping reference values, with substantial local variation and an explicit uncertainty band.
