# Grid Resilience and Fragmentation

## Research Question

Which electrical systems can survive prolonged geopolitical fragmentation, supply-chain disruption, and repeated climate disasters?

## Summary

Advanced civilization depends less on electricity generation in the abstract than on the continued operation of grids, transformers, control systems, maintenance crews, fuel logistics, storage, and replacement-part supply chains.

Project Anthropocene should therefore distinguish between:

- regions with energy resources,
- regions with functioning grids,
- regions with repair capacity,
- and regions with enough institutional stability to coordinate all three.

A solar panel, reactor, dam, turbine, or battery is not a self-sustaining civilization. The decisive variable is whether a community can maintain the wider system around it.

## Core Dependencies

Modern grids depend on:

- high-voltage transformers,
- switchgear,
- power electronics,
- conductors and insulating materials,
- telecommunications,
- software and cybersecurity,
- weather forecasting,
- skilled operators,
- fuel or energy storage,
- and mutual aid across regions.

Large components often have long lead times and specialized manufacturing requirements.

## Resilience Hierarchy

### Tier 1 — Continental Synchronous Grid

Highly efficient and capable of balancing diverse resources across large areas, but dependent on complex coordination and vulnerable to cascading institutional failure.

### Tier 2 — Regional Grid

Can preserve industry, hospitals, transportation, and communications if generation and maintenance remain locally available.

### Tier 3 — Networked Microgrids

Capable of islanding during wider outages and reconnecting when conditions stabilize. Strong candidate for resilient city-states and industrial enclaves.

### Tier 4 — Facility Microgrid

Supports a hospital, data center, farm, military site, water plant, or factory. Valuable but insufficient for a broad urban economy without interconnection.

### Tier 5 — Household or Device Power

Preserves lighting, refrigeration, communications, and small tools but not advanced manufacturing or municipal systems.

## Causal Chain

```text
Repeated disasters and deferred investment
→ outages and equipment losses rise
→ replacement lead times lengthen
→ utilities cannibalize equipment and ration service
→ industrial customers build private microgrids
→ wealthy districts and strategic facilities retain power
→ peripheral areas become less reliable
→ the national grid fragments into regional electrical islands
```

## Generation Types

### Solar and Wind

Modular, increasingly cheap, and useful at many scales. Long-term operation still requires inverters, storage, electronics, replacement blades, lubricants, wiring, and maintenance.

### Hydroelectricity

Can provide durable power and storage where water regimes remain suitable. Dams require inspection, sediment management, mechanical repairs, and political control of river systems.

### Nuclear Fission

Can provide dense, reliable power for regions retaining highly trained staff, regulation, fuel services, cooling water, security, and specialized maintenance. It is unlikely to be maintained casually by isolated settlements.

### Fossil Fuels

Existing plants can remain valuable where extraction and transport survive, but fuel logistics create strategic dependence and emissions remain high.

### Geothermal

Potentially resilient in favorable regions, but drilling and replacement equipment remain industrially demanding.

### Fusion

Fusion should remain provisional. If commercially mature before fragmentation, it may power a small number of high-capacity enclaves. It should not be assumed cheap, compact, or maintenance-free.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Electrical borders become political borders.
- Grid engineers and transformer technicians become strategically important professions.
- Old transmission corridors are defended, repaired, or scavenged.
- City-states form around dependable generation and industrial loads.
- Nighttime satellite imagery of the setting would show isolated constellations rather than continuous national networks.
- Some communities possess abundant generation but cannot use it fully because controls, storage, or transmission have failed.

## Proposed Canon

By the setting era, the old continental grids have largely fragmented into regional systems, protected urban grids, industrial microgrid networks, and low-power rural zones. Advanced societies are distinguished by repair ecosystems rather than generation alone.
