# Fragmentation of Globalism

## Research Question

What sequence of economic, political, technological, and environmental pressures could end the current global order without ending long-distance trade or diplomacy entirely?

## Summary

Globalization is unlikely to vanish in one moment.

It can fragment through repeated export controls, sanctions, wars, debt crises, insurance withdrawal, shipping disruptions, data localization, industrial policy, migration restrictions, and declining trust in international institutions.

The likely outcome is not universal isolation. It is a layered world of regional blocs, protected supply chains, strategic corridors, intermittent global exchange, and areas excluded from high-value trade.

## Systems Most Vulnerable to Fragmentation

- food and fertilizer trade;
- energy and critical minerals;
- semiconductors and precision machinery;
- pharmaceuticals and medical inputs;
- shipping insurance and trade finance;
- cloud computing and data routing;
- aviation and satellite services;
- migration agreements;
- and international disaster assistance.

## Systems Likely to Persist

- regional commodity exchange;
- diplomatic recognition;
- technical standards where compatibility remains valuable;
- scientific and intelligence exchange among aligned powers;
- strategic maritime routes;
- and black or gray markets connecting hostile blocs.

## Causal Chain

```text
Repeated shortages and political shocks
→ states classify more goods as strategic
→ export controls and domestic subsidies expand
→ firms duplicate supply chains inside trusted blocs
→ trade becomes less efficient and more political
→ poorer and unstable regions lose access first
→ global institutions cannot enforce common rules
→ regional systems become the primary scale of governance and commerce
```

## Stages of Fragmentation

### Stage 1 — De-Risking

States subsidize domestic capacity and reduce dependence on geopolitical rivals.

### Stage 2 — Bloc Formation

Standards, payment systems, technology platforms, and trade agreements divide into competing networks.

### Stage 3 — Emergency Nationalization

Governments seize or direct food, energy, transport, communications, and industrial capacity during repeated crises.

### Stage 4 — Functional Regionalism

Cities, provinces, military districts, and neighboring states build practical systems that bypass weakened national or global institutions.

### Stage 5 — Archipelago Globalism

Long-distance trade survives mainly between secure ports, industrial enclaves, strategic corridors, and aligned regional powers.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- A settlement can possess goods from another continent without participating in a unified global economy.
- Ports, rail junctions, river systems, and communications gateways become sovereign power centers.
- Technical standards become instruments of alliance and exclusion.
- Travelers cross economic and digital borders as often as political ones.
- Some old international organizations survive as archives, standards bodies, diplomatic forums, or humanitarian networks.
- Smuggling and neutral trade cities connect blocs that publicly refuse direct exchange.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Every country becoming fully self-sufficient.
- International trade dropping instantly to zero.
- Treating regionalization as the end of all globalization.
- Ignoring finance, standards, insurance, and data networks.
- Assuming international organizations either remain fully powerful or disappear completely.

## Proposed Canon

The old era ends through the fragmentation of universal systems into regional and strategic networks. By the setting era, globalization persists as an archipelago rather than a seamless world market.
