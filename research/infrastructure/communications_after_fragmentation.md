# Communications After Fragmentation

## Research Question

How much of the internet and telecommunications system can survive when states, grids, satellites, cables, and cloud platforms fragment?

## Summary

The internet is resilient because it is distributed, but it is not independent of physical infrastructure or institutions.

It depends on power, fiber routes, undersea cables, data centers, routers, spectrum coordination, domain-name services, satellite ground stations, maintenance ships, replacement electronics, and commercial agreements.

Project Anthropocene should depict communications degrading unevenly into regional networks rather than disappearing everywhere at once.

## Network Layers

### Global Backbone

Undersea cables, major terrestrial fiber corridors, global routing, cloud platforms, satellite constellations, and international standards.

### Regional Internet

National or multi-state fiber backbones, data centers, exchanges, and regional services.

### Local Networks

Municipal fiber, microwave links, private radio, mesh networks, local servers, and community archives.

### Store-and-Forward Networks

Delay-tolerant systems that move messages and datasets through intermittent radio, vehicles, drones, ships, or scheduled satellite links.

## Failure Mechanisms

- grid outages and fuel shortages;
- cable cuts without repair vessels;
- loss of router and optical-component supply;
- political filtering and network partition;
- satellite collision, orbital debris, or ground-station failure;
- abandoned rural infrastructure;
- cyberattack and loss of certificate or identity authorities;
- and collapse of platform businesses that host essential services.

## Causal Chain

```text
Geopolitical rivalry and disasters
→ states localize data and routing
→ global platforms duplicate or withdraw
→ cable and satellite maintenance becomes less reliable
→ regional networks cache essential knowledge
→ intermittent gateways replace continuous global access
→ the internet becomes an archipelago of connected intranets
```

## Resilient Technologies

- terrestrial fiber with redundant routes;
- microwave and high-frequency radio;
- local wireless mesh networks;
- amateur and public-service radio;
- low-Earth-orbit satellites where replacement launches continue;
- high-altitude platforms and drones;
- delay-tolerant networking;
- local-first applications;
- and replicated offline archives.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Messages between distant regions may travel quickly when gateways align, then stop for weeks.
- Global names and addresses become unreliable; local identity systems dominate.
- Cities preserve regional copies of encyclopedias, technical libraries, maps, software, and public records.
- Radio operators, cable technicians, network engineers, and courier services become strategic professions.
- News is delayed, filtered, forged, and politically valuable.
- A surviving global communications layer may exist for governments and industrial powers while ordinary people use local networks.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- A single event instantly erasing the entire internet.
- Global cloud services surviving without power, hardware, staff, and finance.
- Mesh networks replacing high-capacity backbones without major tradeoffs.
- Satellites operating forever without ground control and replenishment.
- Treating communications failure as identical across urban, rural, oceanic, and orbital systems.

## Proposed Canon

By the setting era, the old internet survives as a hierarchy of regional nets, protected industrial backbones, intermittent long-distance gateways, radio systems, and local archives. True continuous global access is rare and politically controlled.
