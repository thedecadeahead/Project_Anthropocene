# Industrial Capacity and the Maintenance Economy

## Research Question

What separates a society that merely possesses old technology from one that can preserve or advance an industrial civilization?

## Summary

Knowledge is necessary but insufficient.

Advanced industry requires mines, refineries, chemical plants, machine tools, metrology, standards, logistics, skilled labor, power, water, spare parts, and institutions capable of coordinating them.

Project Anthropocene should treat maintenance capacity as a central measure of power. A region able to inspect, rebuild, standardize, and manufacture replacement parts can preserve advanced systems far longer than one dependent on inherited stockpiles.

## Industrial Layers

### Extractive Base

Mining, quarrying, forestry, recycling, and access to industrial feedstocks.

### Materials Processing

Steel, aluminum, copper, glass, cement, polymers, ceramics, industrial gases, and specialty chemicals.

### Machine-Building Capacity

Foundries, forges, mills, lathes, precision machining, additive manufacturing, heat treatment, bearings, motors, pumps, and tools.

### High-Complexity Industry

Semiconductors, pharmaceuticals, aerospace, turbines, advanced batteries, optical systems, and scientific instruments.

### Maintenance Institutions

Standards bodies, technical schools, calibration laboratories, inspection systems, documentation archives, and inventories.

## Maintenance Ladder

1. **Operate** — use an existing system.
2. **Service** — perform routine maintenance with stocked consumables.
3. **Repair** — diagnose faults and replace components.
4. **Rebuild** — restore major assemblies using local shops.
5. **Replicate** — manufacture compatible replacements.
6. **Redesign** — adapt systems around available materials and processes.
7. **Advance** — improve performance through research and new production.

Many post-fragmentation societies may remain at different levels for different technologies.

## Causal Chain

```text
Global supply chains become unreliable
→ firms and states stockpile critical components
→ maintenance intervals lengthen and substitution increases
→ standardized regional designs replace diverse consumer products
→ salvage and remanufacturing expand
→ industrial regions specialize and trade strategically
→ technical compatibility becomes a diplomatic issue
```

## Critical Materials

Energy, electronics, motors, batteries, and high-temperature systems depend on materials with concentrated mining or refining. Scarcity does not mean a technology vanishes, but it can make replacement slower, more expensive, or politically controlled.

Recycling becomes increasingly important, although material recovery requires energy, chemistry, sorting, and process control.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Repair manuals, process recipes, calibration standards, and tool libraries are strategic archives.
- Technical colleges may be more important than universities to day-to-day survival.
- Product diversity contracts as regions standardize a smaller set of maintainable designs.
- Salvage crews feed formal remanufacturing industries rather than functioning only as scavengers.
- Industrial diplomacy centers on bearings, catalysts, medicines, power electronics, machine tools, and skilled personnel.
- Some societies preserve old systems beautifully but cannot reproduce them; others use simpler technology they can manufacture indefinitely.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Assuming blueprints alone recreate advanced industry.
- Assuming all manufactured goods disappear when globalization contracts.
- Treating additive manufacturing as able to print any complete machine.
- Ignoring metrology, tolerances, heat treatment, chemistry, and quality control.
- Making every region technologically self-sufficient.

## Proposed Canon

The strongest post-collapse powers are maintenance states: societies that organize education, standards, salvage, manufacturing, and trade around preserving essential systems. Technology level should vary by sector, not be summarized with one universal label.
