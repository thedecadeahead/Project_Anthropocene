# State Capacity and Emergency Rule

## Research Question

How do modern states lose capacity gradually, and how can repeated emergencies transform governance without requiring sudden total collapse?

## Summary

State collapse is usually not a light switch.

A government may retain flags, laws, elections, and international recognition while losing the ability to deliver security, justice, infrastructure, health care, taxation, currency stability, or disaster response across all of its territory.

Project Anthropocene should model state failure as uneven functional retreat. Different agencies, cities, provinces, utilities, militaries, corporations, and mutual-aid systems inherit different parts of governance.

## Dimensions of State Capacity

- **Fiscal:** ability to raise revenue, borrow, and allocate resources.
- **Administrative:** ability to collect information and implement policy.
- **Infrastructural:** ability to maintain power, water, transport, communications, and public works.
- **Security:** ability to control armed force and protect people and trade.
- **Legal:** ability to enforce contracts, rights, and dispute resolution.
- **Social:** ability to provide health, education, food support, and disaster relief.
- **Legitimacy:** public belief that institutions are entitled and able to govern.

A state may remain strong in one dimension and weak in another.

## Causal Chain

```text
Repeated disasters and economic losses
→ emergency spending rises
→ maintenance and social investment are deferred
→ tax bases and public trust weaken
→ services become unequal across territory
→ local governments, utilities, firms, and armed groups fill gaps
→ national authority becomes negotiated rather than assumed
→ emergency arrangements harden into new political systems
```

## Emergency Governance

Repeated crises can normalize:

- rationing,
- movement restrictions,
- compulsory labor,
- requisition of property,
- automated surveillance,
- emergency courts,
- military logistics,
- price controls,
- managed relocation,
- and suspension of ordinary procurement or environmental rules.

Emergency measures can preserve life and infrastructure. They can also entrench corruption, exclusion, and authoritarian rule.

## Functional Retreat

States are likely to abandon functions in a sequence shaped by local conditions.

Possible patterns include:

1. reduced rural service coverage;
2. privatized or municipal utilities;
3. selective infrastructure protection;
4. special economic and security zones;
5. regional tax retention;
6. provincial control of armed forces;
7. ceremonial national government over autonomous territories.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- A country may still exist on maps while travelers encounter different authorities every few hundred kilometers.
- Utilities and transport authorities can become proto-governments.
- Emergency management agencies may evolve into permanent executive institutions.
- Citizenship rights may depend on registration, labor contribution, utility membership, or residence inside protected zones.
- National militaries may transform into regional engineering, logistics, and security organizations.
- Communities may trust a local water board more than a distant national legislature.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Assuming one disaster instantly abolishes the state.
- Treating weak capacity as the absence of all governance.
- Assuming decentralization is automatically democratic or authoritarian.
- Ignoring public legitimacy and service delivery.
- Treating every region of a state as equally governed.

## Proposed Canon

The end of the old global order occurs through prolonged functional retreat. Many successor states retain legal continuity with old nations while real authority shifts to regions, cities, utilities, military districts, cooperatives, and corporations.
