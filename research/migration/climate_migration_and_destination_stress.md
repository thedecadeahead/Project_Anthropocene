# Climate Migration and Destination Stress

## Research Question

How can climate-related mobility reshape cities, borders, labor markets, and political legitimacy without assuming a single global refugee wave?

## Summary

Climate migration is not one phenomenon. It includes temporary evacuation, repeated displacement, rural-to-urban movement, managed retreat, labor migration, seasonal relocation, and permanent cross-border movement.

Most climate-related movement is likely to occur within countries, especially at first. The World Bank's Groundswell modeling estimated that, without strong action, climate change could contribute to as many as 216 million internal migrants across six world regions by 2050. That figure is scenario-dependent and should not be treated as a precise forecast.

The destabilizing mechanism is often not movement itself, but the failure of destination regions to expand housing, water, transport, schools, health care, jobs, and political inclusion quickly enough.

## Migration Types

### Acute Displacement

People flee storms, floods, fires, or heat emergencies. Many return; some are displaced repeatedly until return becomes impossible.

### Slow-Onset Retreat

Households leave regions affected by salinization, water decline, crop loss, coastal erosion, or insurance withdrawal.

### Seasonal Mobility

Workers and households relocate during dangerous heat, drought, smoke, or storm seasons.

### State-Managed Relocation

Governments buy out, rehouse, or compel communities to move.

### Trapped Populations

The poorest, oldest, least healthy, or politically marginalized residents may be unable to move even when conditions worsen.

## Causal Chain

```text
Repeated hazard and livelihood loss
→ household debt and declining local services
→ younger and wealthier residents leave first
→ origin-region tax base and labor force shrink
→ destination housing and infrastructure strain
→ rents, informal settlements, and political backlash grow
→ migrants face exclusion or coercive settlement policies
→ mobility becomes a security issue
```

## Destination Stress

Receiving regions may experience:

- housing shortages,
- rapid land conversion,
- labor-market competition,
- rising infrastructure costs,
- school and health-system crowding,
- water allocation conflicts,
- cultural and linguistic change,
- and political movements demanding exclusion or redistribution.

Migration can also benefit destinations by adding labor, skills, entrepreneurship, tax revenue, and demographic renewal.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Climate-receiving cities become culturally hybrid and politically contested.
- Some governments grant settlement rights in exchange for labor or military service.
- Transport corridors and border cities become powerful institutions.
- Repeatedly displaced populations form mobile cultures and mutual-aid networks.
- Citizenship may become tied to water districts, utilities, employers, or municipalities rather than nation-states.
- Trapped populations remain in regions outsiders mistakenly describe as empty.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Calling every climate migrant a refugee under current international law.
- Treating all movement as international.
- Assuming migration automatically causes conflict.
- Ignoring the agency and economic contribution of migrants.
- Using one global number as a forecast for a fictional future.
- Assuming everyone exposed to climate risk can afford to relocate.

## Proposed Canon

Migration becomes one of the primary engines of cultural and political transformation. The collapse timeline should feature repeated regional movements and destination crises rather than one singular exodus.
