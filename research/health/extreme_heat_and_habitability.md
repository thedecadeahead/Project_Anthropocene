# Extreme Heat and Human Habitability

## Research Question

When does heat make a region dangerous, economically dysfunctional, or seasonally uninhabitable, and what adaptations remain plausible?

## Summary

Habitability is not a single temperature threshold.

Human heat risk depends on air temperature, humidity, solar radiation, wind, exertion, hydration, health, age, clothing, acclimatization, housing, electricity, and access to cooling.

The commonly cited 35°C wet-bulb threshold describes an approximate upper physiological limit under idealized conditions, not the temperature at which ordinary populations first experience mass mortality. Experimental research suggests dangerous limits can occur at lower wet-bulb conditions, especially for older or vulnerable people.

Project Anthropocene should distinguish:

- survivable outdoor conditions,
- safe labor conditions,
- safe indoor conditions,
- infrastructure-supported habitability,
- and unaided biological survivability.

## Core Mechanisms

- Humidity reduces evaporative cooling from sweat.
- Direct sunlight and low wind increase heat load.
- Warm nights prevent physiological recovery.
- Power failure can turn a heat wave into a mass-casualty event.
- Water shortages impair cooling and hydration.
- Outdoor workers, pregnant people, children, older adults, and people with chronic illness face greater risk.
- Repeated heat can reduce labor productivity even below lethal thresholds.

## Habitability Ladder

### Level 1 — Uncomfortable

Daily life continues, but cooling demand and heat illness rise.

### Level 2 — Labor-Constrained

Outdoor work becomes unsafe during substantial portions of the day. Construction, farming, logistics, and military operations shift toward nighttime or mechanization.

### Level 3 — Infrastructure-Dependent

Ordinary life remains possible only with reliable electricity, cooling, water, insulated shelter, and emergency planning.

### Level 4 — Seasonal Retreat

Large populations leave during the hottest months, and only hardened facilities or vulnerable residents remain.

### Level 5 — Recurrent Mass-Casualty Risk

A prolonged grid or water failure during extreme heat can kill large numbers rapidly.

### Level 6 — Persistent Unsheltered Intolerance

Unaided outdoor exposure becomes physiologically intolerable for extended periods.

## Causal Chain

```text
More frequent extreme heat and warm nights
→ cooling demand and health burden rise
→ grid peaks and water demand intensify
→ outages become more damaging
→ employers shift hours and automate labor
→ poorer households face disproportionate mortality
→ seasonal migration and property abandonment increase
→ tax bases and maintenance decline
→ future heat events become deadlier
```

## Adaptation

Plausible adaptations include:

- district cooling,
- passive design,
- reflective materials,
- shade networks,
- urban vegetation where water permits,
- thermal refuges,
- underground or partially buried spaces,
- nighttime economies,
- work-rest regulation,
- cooling garments,
- mechanized outdoor labor,
- and seasonal migration.

Cooling can preserve cities that would otherwise become unsafe, but it creates dependence on power, water, maintenance, refrigerants, and social access.

## Worldbuilding Implications

- Some cities become nocturnal for part of the year.
- Noon curfews and labor restrictions become normal civic institutions.
- Cooling centers evolve into essential public infrastructure.
- Wealthy enclaves remain habitable behind hardened grids while informal settlements suffer repeated mortality.
- Heat-safe transit corridors, shaded markets, and buried utility districts shape architecture.
- The decisive disaster is often not the hottest temperature recorded, but a multi-day heat event combined with grid, water, or governance failure.

## Outcomes to Avoid

- Declaring a whole country permanently uninhabitable based on one threshold.
- Treating wet-bulb temperature as identical to heat index or wet-bulb globe temperature.
- Assuming healthy young adults represent the whole population.
- Ignoring warm nights and indoor heat.
- Assuming air conditioning is either a complete solution or unavailable everywhere.

## Proposed Canon

By the late 21st century, several tropical and subtropical regions should be seasonally infrastructure-dependent, with outdoor labor substantially reorganized. Permanent abandonment should occur mainly where heat combines with water, poverty, conflict, or repeated infrastructure failure.
