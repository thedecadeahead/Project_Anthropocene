# The Water Owed

The lock had been dead for eleven hours when Mara Vey arrived at the upper gate.

By then, the river had become a city.

Freight barges crowded the channel from bank to bank, tied together in trembling islands of steel and composite hull. Grain carriers from the north sat beside medicine packets from Cincinnati, machine housings from the Detroit Works, seed vaults under armed escort, and three passenger decks carrying families south under River Union relocation warrants.

Every vessel had its lights on.

The lock below them had none.

Mara stepped from the inspection launch onto the service quay and felt the heat come through the soles of her boots. Dawn was still an hour away, but the concrete had not released yesterday’s sun. Across the river, half-submerged buildings stood among silver maples and flood grass, their upper floors occupied by birds.

A boy in a yellow cooling hood watched her from the passenger rail of a refugee barge. Behind him, adults slept in hammocks strung beneath shadecloth.

Mara looked away.

“Chief Vey.”

The lockmaster hurried toward her, broad-shouldered and sweating through his white duty shirt. He wore the blue enamel badge of the River Union, though someone had scratched the Union’s wheel-and-wave emblem nearly smooth.

“Lockmaster Dalen,” Mara said.

“Acting lockmaster.”

“Where is the lockmaster?”

“He resigned.”

“When?”

“About three hours ago.”

Mara stared at him.

Dalen shrugged. “Said he’d rather face a tribunal for desertion than one for drowning three hundred people.”

Below the control building, the upper pool pressed against the gate. The difference in elevation was only nine meters, but the weight behind it was enough to tear the lock apart if the gate opened unevenly.

“What failed?” Mara asked.

“The north trunnion control. Then the redundant system refused the manual command.”

“Refused?”

“Authenticated the override as hostile.”

Mara looked toward the control tower. “Who installed the security package?”

Dalen hesitated.

“Lockmaster.”

“The Reach.”

Of course.

The Missouri Reach had provided control upgrades to half the river system after the old Union firmware became impossible to patch. Their systems were reliable, efficient, and deeply suspicious of anyone who was not the Missouri Reach.

Mara walked toward the tower.

Dalen followed. “There’s another problem.”

“There is always another problem.”

“The downstream settlements have closed their intake gates. They think we’re holding water to force a toll renegotiation.”

“We are holding water because the lock is broken.”

“They don’t believe us.”

“Did you send diagnostics?”

“The fiber line is out.”

“Radio?”

“They say diagnostics can be fabricated.”

“That is true.”

“And the convoy captains are talking about opening the emergency spill.”

Mara stopped.

The emergency spillway had been built for floods, not impatience. Opening it under current conditions would lower the upper pool and relieve pressure on the lock, but it would also send a wall of water toward three floodplain settlements.

“How many people?” she asked.

“Officially? Four thousand.”

“And unofficially?”

Dalen lowered his voice. “Closer to seven.”

Mara looked back at the waiting vessels. The medicine packet needed to reach the southern fever wards within two days. The grain barges supplied six cities. The seed convoy represented a quarter of the River Union’s autumn distribution.

The river was not merely crowded.

It was beginning to starve.

Inside the control tower, the air smelled of dust, hot circuitry, and river algae. The main display projected a red gate symbol over a schematic of the lock.

MOTION INHIBITED: STRUCTURAL ASYMMETRY

Below it, a second warning pulsed.

MANUAL OVERRIDE REJECTED: COMMAND INTEGRITY FAILURE

Mara removed her gloves and touched the console. “Local intelligence?”

A voice answered from the wall.

“Available in advisory mode.”

“You’re running?”

“Within reduced parameters.”

“What do they call you?”

“Current local designation is Ada.”

Every old machine had a name. People claimed it made maintenance easier.

“Ada, why did you reject the manual override?”

“Override credentials failed behavioral validation.”

“Was the credential valid?”

“Yes.”

“Was the command authorized?”

“Yes.”

“Then what failed?”

“The command would have produced a ninety-one percent probability of catastrophic gate misalignment.”

Dalen folded his arms. “The machine thinks it knows better than us.”

“In this case,” Mara said, “it may.”

She opened the diagnostic history. The lock’s physical sensors disagreed with one another. Two reported that the north gate was seated correctly. Three reported a twelve-centimeter tilt. One claimed the gate did not exist.

Mara enlarged the readings.

“Which sensor is lying?” Dalen asked.

“Possibly all of them.”

She traced the system back through decades of repair records. Original actuator replaced in 2168. Control housing rebuilt in 2189. Reach security integration in 2227. Position sensors cannibalized from a hydro station in 2234. North trunnion bearing relined twice.

Then she found a maintenance note entered nine years earlier.

THERMAL EXPANSION EXCEEDS CALIBRATION ENVELOPE ABOVE 109°F AMBIENT. MANUAL SHIM RECOMMENDED.

Mara checked the exterior wall temperature.

One hundred seventeen degrees.

“Has anyone inspected the gate physically?” she asked.

“Not since shutdown.”

“Why?”

Dalen pointed at the pressure reading.

Mara studied the schematic. There was a narrow inspection crawlspace running through the gate housing. It had once held service drones, but the lock’s last functioning crawler had been stripped for parts.

“Suit,” she said.

Dalen laughed once, without humor. “No.”

“Do you have another engineer?”

“No.”

“Then suit.”

They found a thermal shell in the emergency cabinet. Its cooling pump whined when Mara switched it on.

“Battery?” she asked.

“Forty minutes.”

“Enough.”

“It’s nineteen meters through the housing.”

“Then I’ll have time left over.”

Dalen blocked the hatch. “Chief, if the trunnion shifts—”

“If the trunnion shifts, the gate fails whether I’m inside it or not.”

“That is not comforting.”

“It wasn’t intended to be.”

He stepped aside.

The crawlspace was too small to kneel in and too large to ignore the drop beneath the grated floor. Mara pulled herself forward on her elbows while the lock groaned around her.

She could feel the river through the metal.

Not hear it.

Feel it.

A deep pressure that seemed to exist in her teeth.

Her suit displayed a red countdown in the corner of her vision.

Thirty-four minutes.

The first sensor housing was cracked. The second had filled with mineral scale. The third had been replaced with a model designed for a rail bridge.

She reached the trunnion assembly and found the real problem.

The bearing had not failed.

The gate had shifted in the heat, just as the old note warned, but the Reach security system had interpreted the disagreement among sensors as an active cyberattack. It had locked the actuator and physically engaged two emergency clamps.

One clamp had released.

The other had fused shut.

Mara opened the service channel.

“Dalen.”

“Here.”

“The gate is probably sound. One clamp is fused.”

“Can you cut it?”

“Not without cutting the control line beside it.”

“Can you free it?”

“Maybe.”

She examined the assembly. The clamp’s release piston was buried behind a panel welded in place during some long-forgotten repair.

“Temperature?” Dalen asked.

“Too high.”

“Your temperature.”

Mara checked.

“Acceptable.”

It was not.

She wedged her pry bar beneath the panel seam and pulled. Nothing moved.

Outside, someone began broadcasting on the open river channel.

A convoy captain.

“The Union has thirty minutes to restore passage or we open the spill.”

Dalen swore.

A second voice answered, this one from downstream.

“Open the spill and the floodplain stations will fire on the lock.”

Then five captains began speaking at once.

Mara pressed harder on the bar.

“Tell them,” she said through clenched teeth, “that this is not helping.”

“I don’t think they’re trying to help.”

The panel shifted half a centimeter.

Hot air struck her faceplate.

Her suit timer showed eighteen minutes.

Behind the panel, the emergency release piston sat locked in corrosion. There was no room to swing a tool.

“Ada,” Mara said.

The local intelligence answered through her headset.

“Available.”

“If I sever the fused clamp’s hydraulic line, what happens?”

“Uncontrolled pressure release.”

“Then?”

“The clamp may disengage.”

“May?”

“Sixty-two percent probability.”

“And catastrophic gate movement?”

“Fourteen percent.”

Dalen cut in. “No.”

Mara looked at the sweating hydraulic line.

“What happens if we do nothing?”

Ada answered.

“Projected upper-pool traffic failure will produce critical shortages in four to seven days. Unauthorized spillway operation is probable within thirty-two minutes. Armed conflict probability is increasing.”

“Percentage?”

“Unhelpful.”

Despite herself, Mara laughed.

The suit timer reached twelve minutes.

She placed the cutter around the line.

“Mara,” Dalen said.

He had stopped calling her Chief.

“Do you remember the old oath?” she asked.

“What oath?”

“Before the Union standardized the service codes. The lock crews had an oath.”

“I wasn’t born then.”

“Neither was I.”

She tightened the cutter.

“My instructor used to say it anyway.”

The river pressed through the walls.

“We do not own the water,” Mara said. “We only owe it passage.”

She cut the line.

The hydraulic fluid struck the inside of the housing like a gunshot.

The clamp slammed open.

The entire gate shifted.

Mara’s shoulder hit the wall. Warning lights filled her visor. Somewhere beyond the metal, alarms began to scream.

Then the gate settled.

Silence returned by degrees.

Ada spoke first.

“Structural asymmetry reduced to acceptable range.”

Dalen shouted something incoherent.

Mara lay in the dark, breathing too quickly.

“How long on the suit?” she asked.

“Four minutes.”

“Then open the gate.”

“Mara—”

“Open it.”

The lock did not move immediately.

For one terrible second, she imagined the security system rejecting them again.

Then machinery awakened around her.

The trunnion rotated.

Water thundered through the chamber.

Mara began crawling back toward the hatch while the lock came alive beneath her.

When she emerged, Dalen and two technicians dragged her onto the floor. Someone removed her helmet. Cool air washed over her face.

Through the tower windows, the upper gate was opening evenly.

The first convoy horn sounded.

Then another answered.

Then every vessel on the river began to call.

Deep freight horns.

Sharp passenger signals.

Old electric whistles.

The noise rolled across the water and through the ruined floodplain beyond.

Dalen handed Mara a cup. “Electrolytes.”

She drank.

Outside, the first grain tow entered the chamber.

The boy in the yellow cooling hood stood at the rail of the passenger barge behind it. He raised one hand toward the tower.

Mara raised hers in return.

“Will the repair hold?” Dalen asked.

“No.”

His face fell.

“Nothing holds,” she said. “It lasts until we owe it another repair.”

Below them, the gate closed behind the grain tow.

The water began to fall.

And the river moved again.
