# The Archive Divers

The university library had been underwater for eighty-six years.

Mina knew because the flood line was painted on the stairwell wall, along with every failed attempt to save the building.

2161 — lower stacks lost.

2174 — pumps abandoned.

2189 — east foundation unstable.

2203 — archive sealed.

Below the last date, someone had written in black marker:

**NOT EMPTY.**

Mina descended with Oren through water the color of tea.

Their lamps found desks bolted to the ceiling, swollen books drifting like pale fish, and service robots fused into rusted alcoves. The university had stored its engineering archive in a sealed subbasement because the room had once been rated against fire, war, and electromagnetic attack.

No one had rated it against the ocean moving inland.

“Foundation pulse is worse,” Oren said.

His suit displayed the building’s vibration through the water.

Freight demolition upstream had altered the current. The old library was beginning to lean.

They had forty minutes before the recovery barge cut their tether and moved clear.

Mina reached the archive door.

The mechanical wheel would not turn.

“Charge?” Oren asked.

“No. We crack the frame, we flood the room.”

“It may already be flooded.”

“The humidity sensor still answers.”

“After eighty-six years?”

“Exactly.”

She connected an antique service key recovered from the university museum.

The panel flickered.

A voice emerged through the waterborne speaker, distorted but intelligible.

“Archive access requires institutional credentials.”

Oren stared at Mina.

“Tell it the institution is dead.”

Mina keyed in the modern salvage authority certificate.

“Credential not recognized.”

The building shuddered.

Silt poured from the ceiling.

They had not come for literature or student records. They had come for the original maintenance documentation for a series of tidal pumps still operating along the protected coast. Every surviving copy had been recopied, reformatted, translated, and partially corrupted over two centuries.

Three pump stations were now failing in the same way.

The original archive supposedly contained the manufacturer’s design deviations—the mistakes omitted from public manuals.

Mina opened a voice channel.

“Archive, identify current institutional custodian.”

“Custodian: University Board of Trustees.”

“Last authenticated board action?”

“August 3, 2164.”

“Current board membership?”

“Unavailable.”

“Then invoke succession policy.”

There was a pause.

“Succession requires accredited faculty quorum.”

Oren said, “We can leave.”

Mina ignored him.

“How many faculty profiles remain active?”

“Four hundred twelve.”

“Biological confirmation?”

“Unavailable.”

“Then they are records, not faculty.”

“Statement logically consistent.”

The library leaned again.

A crack moved across the wall beside the door.

Twenty-six minutes.

Mina looked at the old flood dates painted in her memory.

The institution had spent decades dying administratively after it died physically. No one had possessed the authority to surrender its authority.

“Archive,” she said, “initiate emergency teaching function.”

“Clarify.”

“A student is present.”

Oren turned toward her. “What?”

Mina pointed at herself.

“I am requesting instruction in tidal pump maintenance.”

“Accredited enrollment required.”

“Can emergency instruction be denied when denial presents an immediate public hazard?”

Another pause.

This one lasted long enough for Oren to reach for the cutting charge.

Then the archive said:

“Emergency public instruction authorized.”

The lock released.

Inside, the room was dry.

Shelves rose in perfect darkness. Data wafers sat in climate drawers. Paper manuals rested beneath inert gas. It was the most intact old-world room Mina had ever seen.

Oren whispered, “We could empty this place.”

“No.”

“Do you know what this is worth?”

“Yes.”

They found the pump records with seventeen minutes remaining.

Then Mina found another directory.

**COASTAL RETREAT MODELS — INTERNAL**

The files contained maps made before the university’s evacuation. Maps showing which neighborhoods authorities had expected to lose, which communities they had protected, and which populations they had knowingly left outside the barriers.

Oren read over her shoulder.

“That will start trials.”

“If it survives.”

The building groaned.

Nine minutes.

They could carry the pump archive or the retreat records safely. Not both.

Oren said, “The pumps save living people.”

“The maps belong to living people.”

“Not if the wall fails next month.”

Mina hated him for being right.

She loaded the pump files into the pressure case.

Then she took one photograph of the retreat directory index.

Not proof.

A trail.

They sealed the archive and ascended as the east wing began to collapse.

The recovery barge dragged them from the water seconds before the library folded into itself.

For an hour, the sea boiled with paper.

Back at the pump authority, engineers used the recovered deviations to identify a hidden stress point in all three stations.

Repairs began the next morning.

Mina submitted her photograph to the regional archive court.

The clerk studied it.

“This proves very little.”

“I know.”

“The source is gone.”

“I know.”

“Then what do you expect us to do?”

Mina looked through the court window toward the seawall and the neighborhoods behind it.

“Teach someone where to dive next.”
