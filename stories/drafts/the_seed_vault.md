# The Seed Vault

The seed case weighed less than a child and mattered more than a town.

Ilya carried it chained to his wrist.

He and Nessa had been moving north for three days through fields that looked healthy from the road. Green leaves. Tall stalks. Clean rows under autonomous irrigation rigs.

But every farmer they passed watched the sky instead of the crop.

The wheat had flowered during the heat spike.

Most of the heads were empty.

At the ruined interstate exchange outside Fulton, a militia stopped them beneath the skeleton of an old toll canopy.

Their captain was a woman with silver cooling tubes braided through her hair.

“Open the case,” she said.

“No,” Ilya answered.

She smiled. “I know what’s in it.”

“Then you know why I won’t.”

The case contained two thousand sealed embryos of Reach-17 winter wheat, bred over nineteen years to tolerate hot planting soils, erratic freezes, and short grain-filling seasons. It was not a miracle crop. It still needed water, nitrogen, healthy soil, and luck.

But six cooperatives had lost their seed multipliers in the same summer.

Without Reach-17, they would plant feed grain next year and import bread grain at convoy prices.

If convoys came.

The captain gestured toward the fields.

“My district has eleven thousand people.”

“The treaty allocation goes north.”

“The treaty was written before our harvest failed.”

“So was everyone else’s.”

Nessa stepped between them. “There are duplicate samples.”

“Where?”

“St. Louis. Omaha. Two in the lake country.”

The captain’s smile vanished. “St. Louis lost cooling for six hours last month. Omaha’s rail line is cut. And the lake country does not answer requests from people who cannot pay.”

That, Ilya knew, was true.

Behind the militia, families had gathered beneath shadecloth. Not soldiers. Children. Older people. Farm workers in dust-white boots.

The captain said, “Give us one tray.”

“There are only eight.”

“One tray can rebuild our stock.”

“One tray missing means one treaty district gets nothing.”

“Then choose one farther away.”

Ilya felt the chain against his wrist.

He had spent his whole adult life believing systems failed because people ignored rules.

Now the rules had led him to a roadblock where every choice was theft.

A drone buzzed overhead.

Nessa looked up sharply.

Not militia hardware. Reach surveillance.

The captain saw it too.

“You called them?” she asked.

“No,” Ilya said.

The drone broadcast a mechanical voice.

“Custodians, remain in place. Strategic biological material will be recovered by authority of the Reservoir Security Directorate.”

Nessa swore.

The captain laughed once. “There is your treaty.”

Two armored carriers appeared on the western road.

The Directorate did not intend to deliver the seed north or leave it here. It intended to place the entire stock under military control.

Nessa pulled Ilya behind a concrete divider as the militia opened fire on the drone.

The exchange filled with noise.

“Can the case survive a round?” she shouted.

“Maybe.”

“Can it survive them taking it?”

“No.”

The Directorate carriers slowed at the barricade.

Ilya unlocked the case.

Inside, eight white trays glowed beneath the thermal monitor.

Nessa stared at him. “What are you doing?”

“Breaking custody.”

“That ends your career.”

“If they take it, no one gets a career.”

He lifted out the first tray and handed it to her.

“North district.”

A second to the militia captain.

“Your district.”

The captain hesitated, then took it.

Ilya distributed the remaining trays among six people in the crowd.

A teacher. A mechanic. A girl no older than fourteen. Two farmers. An old man with a cargo bicycle.

“Different roads,” he said. “Different names. Do not tell each other where you go.”

The Directorate voice boomed from the carriers.

“Surrender controlled material immediately.”

The captain raised her rifle.

Ilya closed the empty case and chained it back to his wrist.

Then he ran east.

The carriers followed him.

That was the useful thing about an official case. Authority always chased the container before asking what remained inside.

He made it two kilometers before they caught him.

They forced him face-down onto hot pavement and cut the chain.

An officer opened the case.

For several seconds, no one spoke.

“Where is it?”

Ilya turned his face against the road and smiled.

“Growing,” he said.

The officer struck him.

Months later, in a detention block beside a shrinking reservoir, Ilya received a legal notice with no return address.

It contained only eight germination percentages.

87.

91.

84.

93.

89.

76.

90.

88.

Below them, someone had written:

**No one owns a harvest before it grows.**
