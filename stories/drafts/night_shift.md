# Night Shift

At 02:13, the city was awake.

Markets glowed beneath white shade roofs. Children played in mist corridors. Delivery crawlers hummed through streets that would be empty by sunrise.

Above them, Line Crew Nine climbed Tower 6 with the temperature still at one hundred six degrees.

Jo Renn locked her boots to the lattice and looked down.

The city spread below like a circuit board: cooling shelters, apartment blocks, greenhouse roofs, battery courts, and the bright blue lines of the public water loops.

Beyond the protected districts, the old city was dark.

“Fault is on feeder six,” said Malik through the crew channel. “Load is climbing faster than forecast.”

“It always does on festival night,” Jo said.

“The festival was canceled.”

“People still use the lights.”

The feeder supplied three cooling districts and Saint Orra Hospital. Its insulation had begun failing during the previous heat cycle. Replacement cable had been promised twice and redirected twice—first to the desalination pumps, then to the government quarter.

So Crew Nine had patched it.

Then patched the patch.

Now the conductor was arcing against the tower frame.

Jo reached the damaged span and saw the blue flare before her visor dimmed.

“Cut the feeder.”

Dispatch answered immediately. “Denied. Hospital transfer is incomplete.”

“Then the tower may burn.”

“Estimated transfer time: eleven minutes.”

The arc snapped again.

Malik climbed beside her. Sweat floated inside his face seal.

“We don’t have eleven.”

Below, thousands of cooling units pulled against the grid.

At sunrise, the outside temperature would pass one hundred twenty-two. Apartments in the oldest districts stored heat through the day. Without night cooling, people would begin the morning already sick.

Jo opened the load map.

There was one way to keep the hospital and save the tower.

It would require dropping District Twelve.

District Twelve had no hospital, no government buildings, and no industrial contract.

It had eighty thousand people.

Dispatch said, “Crew Nine, hold position.”

Malik studied Jo’s face. “They’re deciding who gets dark.”

“They already decided,” she said.

The order arrived.

“Isolate District Twelve. Preserve medical and civic load.”

Jo did not move.

“Confirm,” Dispatch said.

Malik whispered, “My mother’s in Twelve.”

Jo knew.

Everyone on Crew Nine had someone in Twelve.

That was why crews were usually assigned outside their home districts. But the city had lost too many line workers that summer.

The damaged conductor spat molten metal.

Jo opened a private channel to the district maintenance net.

“Twelve control, this is Crew Nine. Can you shed residential cooling by block?”

A tired voice answered. “Controls are too old. All or nothing.”

“What local generation?”

“Rooftop solar sleeping. Four neighborhood batteries at twenty percent.”

“Water pumps?”

“Grid-tied.”

Jo looked toward the eastern horizon. Dawn was a pale stain beneath the smoke.

“What if you wake the solar early?” Malik asked.

“No direct sun.”

“Not for power. For synchronization.”

She understood.

The district’s inverters could form a local grid if they believed a stable source existed. The batteries could pretend to be that source, but the firmware required a solar reference.

It was an old anti-islanding rule from a safer century.

“Can you spoof sunrise?” Jo asked.

Twelve control went silent.

Then: “That is profoundly illegal.”

“So is dying politely.”

Malik laughed despite himself.

They had six minutes.

Jo uploaded a false irradiance curve through the maintenance port. Across District Twelve, thousands of rooftop inverters woke in darkness.

The batteries synchronized.

The water pumps shifted local.

Cooling systems began dropping room by room, preserving clinics, shelters, and communal halls.

Dispatch shouted in her ear.

“Crew Nine, unauthorized grid formation detected.”

“District Twelve is islanding,” Jo said.

“You are not authorized to create an independent grid.”

“Then authorize it.”

The arc grew white.

Jo cut feeder six.

The tower went dark.

For one breathless second, so did District Twelve.

Then lights returned below in scattered constellations.

Not every building.

Enough.

Saint Orra Hospital remained bright on the main grid.

The tower stopped burning.

At sunrise, Crew Nine descended into a city under heat curfew.

Officials were waiting at the base.

Jo expected arrest.

Instead, an old woman pushed through them and struck Malik across the shoulder.

“You shut off my bedroom,” she said.

“Ma—”

“But the shelter stayed cold.”

She pulled him into an embrace.

Around them, residents from Twelve filled the service road carrying water, tools, breakfast packets, and handwritten load maps.

They had spent the final hours of darkness deciding which buildings would share power through the day.

Dispatch sent Jo a formal suspension notice.

A second message arrived from Twelve control.

**LOCAL GRID STABLE. REQUEST TECHNICAL ASSISTANCE WITH PERMANENT ISLANDING.**

Jo looked at the city’s official map.

For the first time, District Twelve appeared with its own electrical boundary.

Not a blackout.

A border.
