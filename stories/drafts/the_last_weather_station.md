# The Last Weather Station

The mountain sent its first warning as a number.

Pressure falling six millibars in forty minutes.

Sera Vale read it twice, then crossed the observatory to wake Tomas.

He slept beneath three blankets beside the battery wall, one hand curled around the wrench he had been using before dawn. The station had once held twelve technicians. Now there were two of them, one local intelligence with a failing voice module, and enough spare parts to survive either three years or three weeks depending on what broke next.

“Tomas.”

He opened one eye. “Fire?”

“Storm.”

“Storms don’t need me awake.”

“This one does.”

On the western display, the model had painted a red crescent across the high passes. The convoy from the lake country was already two days overdue. Twenty-three trucks, three passenger carriers, a mobile clinic, and seed stock promised to six dryland cooperatives.

The old satellite feed showed only fragments. Half the orbital weather layer had gone dark during the debris season, and the regional network had been intermittent for nine days.

Tomas sat up. “How bad?”

“Bad enough that the pass closes by noon.”

“Forecast confidence?”

“Forty-one percent.”

He stared at her.

Sera said, “The ridge instruments disagree.”

“Of course they do.”

They climbed to the mast in thermal shells while dawn bled red across the snowless peaks. The air was too warm for the season. That made storms harder to read. Moisture came farther inland now, then broke against the mountains in violent, unfamiliar ways.

The west anemometer had frozen in place despite the heat. Its bearing was packed with ash blown from fires hundreds of kilometers away.

Tomas pried it open.

“You trust the model?” he asked.

“I trust the pressure.”

“The model says snow.”

“The air says rain first.”

“Then ice.”

“Then snow.”

He looked toward the pass. “That convoy won’t make it.”

“They will if we turn them south.”

“The southern road crosses the wash country.”

“Dry for now.”

“For now.”

The station intelligence crackled in Sera’s earpiece.

“Regional courier beacon acquired.”

“Patch it through.”

A woman’s voice arrived beneath static.

“Summit Station, this is Convoy Six. We have limited route data and two cooling failures. Request pass status.”

Sera looked at Tomas.

He shook his head once.

“Convoy Six,” Sera said, “primary pass will become impassable before noon. Recommend immediate diversion to the southern wash route.”

Silence.

Then: “The wash route adds nine hours.”

“Yes.”

“We have patients on temperature-controlled medication.”

“If you stay on the primary, you may lose the road entirely.”

Another silence, longer this time.

“Confidence?” the convoy leader asked.

Sera looked at the corrupted satellite image, the faulty ridge instruments, the falling pressure, and the sky.

“High enough to risk being wrong.”

The convoy turned south.

At midday, rain struck the pass in sheets. By one, the temperature fell twenty-two degrees. Water froze across the old pavement. At two, the mountains disappeared behind snow and airborne ice.

The pass sensors went dark one by one.

Tomas watched the outage map spread. “You saved them.”

“We redirected them into a flood channel.”

“The wash is dry.”

“It was dry.”

At dusk, the southern gauge reported rising water.

Sera closed her eyes.

The storm had crossed farther south than the model predicted.

They spent the next hour calling Convoy Six and hearing nothing.

Then the station intelligence spoke.

“Store-and-forward packet received.”

The convoy leader’s voice filled the observatory.

“Summit, we reached Marker Seventeen before the wash rose. Road behind us is gone. Road ahead is passable. We are sheltering under the solar cut until morning.”

Tomas laughed so hard he had to sit down.

Sera leaned against the console.

The message continued.

“Your forecast was wrong.”

Tomas stopped laughing.

Then the woman added, “It was also right enough. Thank you.”

Outside, the storm tore one of the station’s old dishes from its mount. It vanished into the dark.

Tomas looked at the empty status panel.

“That was the last long-range receiver.”

“I know.”

“No replacement until spring.”

“If the convoy makes it.”

He nodded toward the black windows. “What do we do without the receiver?”

Sera began writing down pressure readings by hand.

“The same thing people did before receivers.”

“Guess?”

“Observe.”

At dawn, the storm cleared.

The mountain stood white above a brown and flooded world.

Far below, tiny convoy lights moved east in a patient line.

Sera climbed to the roof and raised the old signal mirror toward them.

One by one, the trucks flashed back.

The station had lost a dish, three sensors, and most of its reach.

But the forecast had crossed the mountain.
