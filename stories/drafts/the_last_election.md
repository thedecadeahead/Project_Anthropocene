# The Last Election

No one under sixty remembered an election that mattered.

The city still held them every four years. Ballots were printed. District halls opened. Candidates made speeches beneath the emergency banners.

But the Emergency Steward had remained in office for thirty-seven years.

There was always a reason.

A heat season. A food shortage. A border closure. A fever outbreak. A grid attack. A flood upriver.

This year, the reason was supposed to end.

Lio Haran stood inside Polling Hall Seven at 05:00 while technicians tested the paper scanners.

“Network verification?” he asked.

“Offline,” said the clerk.

“Good.”

“Central authority requested live reporting.”

“They can request the moon.”

The clerk smiled nervously.

Outside, voters had already formed a line beneath cooling canopies. Some wore Steward blue. Others wore the green sash of Mara Quill, the first opposition candidate permitted to contest the office in twelve years.

At sunrise, the heat curfew began.

The city moved indoors.

Voting continued.

At 09:40, Hall Three lost power.

At 10:15, the Steward’s office announced a suspected coordinated attack.

At 10:22, armored civic vehicles appeared outside Hall Seven.

Lio met their commander at the door.

“Emergency order,” she said. “Polling is suspended pending security review.”

“Show me the order.”

She held up a sealed tablet.

Lio read it twice.

The authority cited the Continuity Statute, written after the famine riots. It allowed the Steward to suspend public gatherings during an infrastructure attack.

It did not mention elections.

“This order is unlawful,” Lio said.

The commander lowered her voice. “Perhaps. Open the door.”

Behind him, two hundred voters waited in silence.

Lio had served the election office for twenty-nine years. Most of that time, his work had been ceremonial. He trained clerks, stored ballots, updated dead district maps, and watched emergency decrees turn temporary exceptions into permanent architecture.

He had told himself preserving the procedure mattered.

One day, someone might use it.

That day had arrived, and procedures were very small things beside armored vehicles.

Then an old woman in line began to sing.

Not an anthem. A children’s counting song.

One for water.

Two for grain.

Three for labor.

Four for rain.

Others joined her.

The commander looked past Lio.

“Do not make this a riot.”

“They are singing.”

“It becomes a riot when headquarters says it is.”

Lio closed the door.

The commander struck it once with her palm.

“Director Haran.”

He locked the manual bar.

Inside, the clerk whispered, “What now?”

“We vote.”

For three hours, Hall Seven operated under siege.

People entered through the service courtyard in groups of five. Ballots went into transparent boxes when the scanners lost power. Volunteers carried drinking water from the shelter tanks.

At noon, the city network disappeared.

At twelve-thirty, neighborhood radio reported that nine halls had closed and fourteen remained open.

At one, the Steward addressed the city.

“My first duty is continuity,” he said. “Until the attack is resolved, no result can be trusted.”

Mara Quill answered by radio from an unknown location.

“A government that cannot survive a vote has already failed continuity.”

At 15:00, the outside temperature reached one hundred twenty-six degrees.

The armored crews began rotating into Hall Seven’s shade.

Lio ordered volunteers to give them water.

The commander accepted a cup without meeting his eyes.

“You know he will not leave,” she said.

“Then he will be removed.”

“By whom?”

Lio gestured toward the ballot boxes.

She almost laughed.

“Paper?”

“Witnesses.”

By evening, returns moved across the city by bicycle, maintenance cart, and foot. Every packet carried duplicate tallies signed by voters, clerks, observers, and sometimes security personnel.

There was no central count.

There were hundreds of local counts, copied too widely to erase.

At midnight, Hall Seven received results from thirty-one districts.

Quill led by six percent.

The Steward’s office declared all results invalid.

The water authority recognized them.

Then the eastern grid board.

Then the transit cooperative.

At 02:00, Saint Iven Hospital announced that it would accept lawful instructions from the certified winner.

At 03:10, the city security commander entered Hall Seven alone.

She removed her blue emergency badge and placed it on the table.

“The Steward has ordered arrests.”

No one moved.

“I have declined.”

Lio looked at the badge.

“Will the rest of your command?”

“Some.”

“Is that enough?”

“No.”

The answer frightened him more than a lie would have.

At dawn, the Steward addressed the city again.

This time, there were no emergency banners behind him.

He looked very old.

“For thirty-seven years,” he said, “I have preserved this city through conditions its founders did not imagine.”

That was true.

“I do not recognize the legality of yesterday’s process.”

Lio’s hands tightened around the tally sheet.

“However,” the Steward continued, “I recognize that the institutions required to govern have chosen another authority.”

Across Hall Seven, no one breathed.

“I will transfer operational control at noon.”

The feed ended.

People began cheering, but Lio sat down.

The clerk touched his shoulder. “We won.”

“No,” he said.

She stared at him.

“We counted.”

At noon, Mara Quill took the oath in the water authority’s assembly chamber because the old civic hall was still under emergency seal.

Her first act was to abolish the Continuity Statute.

Her second was to replace it with a narrower emergency law.

Lio read the draft that evening.

It was better written.

It still contained exceptions.

Outside, workers removed thirty-seven years of blue banners from the streets.

The old woman from the polling line found Lio on the steps.

“Was that the last election?” she asked.

He looked at the city: patched grids, guarded reservoirs, crowded cooling halls, and citizens who had learned that institutions survived only when enough people insisted on using them.

“No,” he said.

“That was the first one.”
