# Source Quality Rubric

This rubric helps Researchers judge whether a source is suitable for supporting Project Anthropocene.

A source does not need to score perfectly to be useful. The goal is to make strengths and weaknesses explicit.

## Evaluation Dimensions

Score each dimension from 0 to 3.

### 1. Authority

- **3 — Strong:** Relevant subject-matter experts, major assessment body, reputable scientific institution, or peer-reviewed research team.
- **2 — Moderate:** Credible professional or institutional source with relevant expertise.
- **1 — Limited:** Generalist source, unclear expertise, or secondary reporting.
- **0 — Weak:** Anonymous, deceptive, promotional, or demonstrably unreliable source.

### 2. Methodological Transparency

- **3 — Strong:** Methods, data, assumptions, and limitations are clearly documented.
- **2 — Moderate:** Most methods are described, but important details are incomplete.
- **1 — Limited:** Conclusions are presented with minimal methodological detail.
- **0 — Weak:** No usable explanation of how claims were produced.

### 3. Evidence Quality

- **3 — Strong:** Uses appropriate data, multiple lines of evidence, validated models, or well-supported historical documentation.
- **2 — Moderate:** Evidence is credible but limited in scale, replication, or applicability.
- **1 — Limited:** Relies heavily on anecdote, selective evidence, or unsupported synthesis.
- **0 — Weak:** Evidence is absent, fabricated, or contradicted by stronger sources.

### 4. Relevance

- **3 — Direct:** Directly addresses the question, region, system, and time horizon.
- **2 — Related:** Relevant but requires careful translation across geography, sector, or period.
- **1 — Indirect:** Useful mainly for analogy or background.
- **0 — Irrelevant:** Does not materially support the claim being made.

### 5. Recency

- **3 — Current:** Reflects the latest major assessment, dataset release, or state of knowledge.
- **2 — Recent:** Still valid, though newer work may refine it.
- **1 — Aging:** Potentially useful historically, but likely outdated in important respects.
- **0 — Superseded:** Replaced or invalidated by stronger evidence.

Recency should not be treated mechanically. Older historical sources and foundational research may remain essential.

### 6. Independence and Bias

- **3 — Strong:** Funding, interests, and conflicts are disclosed; analysis is substantially independent.
- **2 — Moderate:** Some institutional interest exists but methods and evidence remain credible.
- **1 — Limited:** Significant advocacy, commercial, political, or ideological incentives shape presentation.
- **0 — Weak:** Undisclosed conflicts, propaganda, or deliberate manipulation are evident.

## Suggested Interpretation

Maximum score: 18.

- **15–18: Foundational** — suitable for major research conclusions and canon support.
- **11–14: Strong supporting source** — useful with normal caveats.
- **7–10: Contextual** — useful for leads, examples, or comparison; corroboration required.
- **3–6: Weak** — use cautiously and never as sole support for important canon.
- **0–2: Reject** — should not support project claims.

The numeric score is a prompt for judgment, not a substitute for it.

## Red Flags

Researchers should pause when a source:

- makes a precise long-range prediction without uncertainty,
- uses a model outside its intended scope,
- presents one extreme scenario as inevitable,
- confuses local effects with global outcomes,
- cites a press release instead of the underlying research,
- relies on a single graph without methodology,
- uses emotionally charged claims unsupported by evidence,
- omits scenario assumptions,
- treats correlation as causation,
- or repeats a widely shared claim that cannot be traced to an original source.

## Source Roles

Sources should be evaluated according to the role they play.

### Foundational Sources

These establish the broad scientific or historical baseline. Examples include major assessment reports, authoritative datasets, and robust synthesis studies.

### Domain Sources

These answer narrower questions in agriculture, infrastructure, migration, technology, conflict, and other fields.

### Case Studies

These provide detailed evidence from a particular region, event, institution, or population. They should not be generalized without explanation.

### Contemporary Reporting

Journalism can document policies, public reaction, corporate behavior, and unfolding events. It should usually link back to stronger technical sources for scientific claims.

### Speculative and Foresight Work

Scenario planning and foresight studies can identify possibilities but should not be mistaken for forecasts. Their assumptions are part of the evidence record.

## Recording an Evaluation

Use a short table in research notes:

| Dimension | Score | Rationale |
|---|---:|---|
| Authority | 3 | Relevant expert assessment body |
| Methodology | 3 | Methods and limitations documented |
| Evidence | 2 | Strong evidence with regional limits |
| Relevance | 3 | Directly addresses the research question |
| Recency | 3 | Latest available assessment |
| Independence | 2 | Institutional interest disclosed |
| **Total** | **16/18** | Foundational |

## Conflicting Sources

When credible sources disagree:

1. Confirm they are answering the same question.
2. Compare scenarios, definitions, geography, and time periods.
3. Examine methods and data quality.
4. Record the disagreement rather than hiding it.
5. Prefer ranges or conditional outcomes when the evidence does not justify a single answer.

Project Anthropocene should preserve legitimate uncertainty rather than manufacture false certainty.
