# Research Methodology

Project Anthropocene uses real-world evidence to construct a plausible future history. Research does not eliminate uncertainty, but it makes assumptions visible and gives the setting a defensible foundation.

## Research Standard

Researchers should prefer sources in roughly this order:

1. Peer-reviewed scientific literature.
2. Major scientific assessments and synthesis reports.
3. Government and intergovernmental datasets and technical reports.
4. University and research-institute publications.
5. Industry technical documentation and credible professional analysis.
6. High-quality journalism used primarily for contemporary context.
7. Secondary summaries used to locate stronger primary sources.

Popular media, unsourced claims, and sensational summaries should not serve as the sole basis for major canon.

## Recommended Institutions

Depending on the subject, useful source institutions may include:

- Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change,
- United Nations agencies,
- World Meteorological Organization,
- Food and Agriculture Organization,
- NASA,
- NOAA,
- national geological and meteorological agencies,
- public-health agencies,
- national academies,
- universities,
- and peer-reviewed journals.

Institutional authority alone does not make a claim correct. Researchers should still examine scope, date, methodology, uncertainty, and relevance.

## The Evidence Chain

Each major setting claim should be developed through an evidence chain.

### 1. Observation

What is already occurring or directly measured?

Examples include:

- warming trends,
- sea-level rise,
- crop-yield sensitivity,
- groundwater depletion,
- migration patterns,
- infrastructure failures,
- and insurance retreat.

### 2. Projection

What do credible models or assessments project under stated assumptions?

The relevant emissions pathway, time horizon, geography, and confidence level should be recorded.

### 3. Historical Analogy

How have societies responded to comparable pressures in the past?

Historical analogy can inform social and political outcomes, but should not be treated as proof that history will repeat.

### 4. Extrapolation

What fictional development is inferred from the observations, projections, and analogies?

This is where worldbuilding begins. The assumptions connecting evidence to canon should be explicit.

### 5. Consequences

What secondary effects follow from the extrapolation?

Researchers should consider effects on:

- population,
- food,
- water,
- health,
- energy,
- infrastructure,
- trade,
- governance,
- conflict,
- culture,
- and technology.

## Claim Classification

Research notes should label important claims as:

- **Observed** — directly measured or documented.
- **Projected** — produced by a model, scenario, or formal assessment.
- **Inferred** — a reasoned conclusion drawn from multiple sources.
- **Speculative** — plausible but not strongly supported.
- **Fictional Canon** — an accepted setting decision.

A single statement may involve more than one category, but the transition between them should be clear.

## Scenario Discipline

Climate and socioeconomic projections depend on assumptions.

Researchers should record:

- the scenario or pathway used,
- the publication date,
- the modeled time period,
- the geographic scale,
- the reported uncertainty,
- and any important limitations.

Project Anthropocene should not casually combine incompatible worst-case assumptions from unrelated models.

When choosing a severe outcome, explain why the causal conditions required for that outcome exist in the timeline.

## Time Horizons

Confidence generally declines as the timeline extends.

### Near Future

Near-future canon should remain closely tied to current trends, established policy, and credible projections.

### Mid Future

Mid-future canon may use broader extrapolation, especially for institutional change, migration, technology, and conflict.

### Far Future

Far-future canon should focus on internally consistent consequences rather than false precision.

By the setting's present, historical records may be incomplete and regional divergence substantial.

## Technology Research

Technological development should avoid both stagnation and magical progress.

For each major technology, consider:

- current maturity,
- physical constraints,
- resource requirements,
- manufacturing dependencies,
- maintenance burden,
- deployment cost,
- political incentives,
- failure modes,
- and unequal access.

A technology may be scientifically possible without becoming widely available.

## Social and Political Research

Climate models do not directly predict governments, wars, ideologies, or cultures.

Social extrapolation should draw from:

- political science,
- economics,
- sociology,
- anthropology,
- public health,
- disaster studies,
- conflict studies,
- and historical case studies.

Researchers should avoid treating any population as passive, irrational, or culturally static.

## Citation Practice

Research documents should include enough citation detail for another Researcher to locate the source.

At minimum, record:

- author or institution,
- title,
- publication,
- date,
- stable link or identifier,
- and relevant page, table, figure, or section when available.

Direct quotations should be brief and used only when exact wording matters.

## Research Notes Template

A research note should generally include:

```markdown
# Topic

## Question
What are we trying to establish?

## Summary
What does the evidence currently support?

## Key Findings
- Finding one.
- Finding two.

## Uncertainty
What is disputed, unknown, or model-dependent?

## Worldbuilding Implications
What plausible setting developments follow?

## Proposed Canon
What, if anything, should become canon?

## Sources
- Full citation.
```

## Corrections

Project Anthropocene should change when the evidence changes.

Corrections are not failures. A transparent revision based on stronger evidence improves the setting and preserves trust.
