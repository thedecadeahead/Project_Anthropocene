# Climate Evidence Timeline: 2025-2100

**Status:** Research scaffold, not canon history.

This document organizes assessed climate expectations into time bands. It deliberately avoids inventing named disasters, wars, or political outcomes before those are separately researched.

## 2025-2035: Locked-In Near-Term Change

### Strongly Supported

- Global warming continues in the near term under all major emissions scenarios.
- Additional warming intensifies heat extremes and raises the probability of compound hazards.
- Sea level continues rising because of ocean warming and land-ice loss.
- Adaptation spending, disaster recovery, insurance stress, and infrastructure exposure continue increasing.

### Project Interpretation

This decade should still resemble the contemporary world. Globalization remains functional, technological development continues, and climate damage is experienced mainly as repeated regional emergencies, rising costs, and political conflict over response.

## 2035-2050: Adaptation Becomes Structural

### Strongly Supported

- Differences among emissions scenarios remain smaller in the near term than later in the century.
- A global warming level near 1.5°C is reached or exceeded in long-term averages under assessed pathways.
- Coastal flood frequency rises as mean sea level increases.
- Heat exposure, drought risk, heavy precipitation, wildfire weather, and agricultural stress intensify unevenly by region.

### Project Interpretation

Climate adaptation ceases to be treated as a temporary emergency program and becomes a permanent function of government, finance, construction, agriculture, health, and migration policy.

This period is a plausible beginning for:

- large managed-retreat programs,
- routine seasonal work restrictions,
- restructuring of insurance markets,
- strategic food and water reserves,
- and major investment in cooling, desalination, protected agriculture, and grid resilience.

## 2050-2075: Divergence and Compounding Risk

### Assessed Direction

- Climate outcomes diverge more sharply between mitigation pathways.
- Every additional increment of warming increases the severity and concurrency of hazards.
- Adaptation limits become more visible in ecosystems, low-income regions, exposed coasts, and heat-stressed settlements.
- Sea-level rise and ice loss continue regardless of short-term political cycles.

### Project Interpretation

This is the earliest period in which Project Anthropocene's distinctive future begins to diverge substantially from ordinary current-policy projections.

Repeated shocks may interact through:

```text
crop loss
→ price spikes
→ export restrictions
→ unrest and migration
→ emergency spending
→ deferred infrastructure maintenance
→ greater damage during the next shock
```

No singular apocalypse is required. The defining change is declining recovery capacity between disasters.

## 2075-2100: Severe Climate, Uneven Institutions

### Assessed Direction

Under intermediate-to-high pathways, late-century warming is substantially higher than today, with much larger risks to food, water, ecosystems, health, and settlements.

Global mean sea level continues to rise and local relative rise may be much greater in subsiding regions.

### Project Baseline

The current working scenario uses a late-century central warming value near 3.2°C above 1850-1900, with a planning range of 2.5-4.0°C.

This is a Project Anthropocene inference, not a direct official forecast.

By this period, the project expects severe fragmentation of global coordination to be plausible, but political collapse is not yet established as canon. Separate research must determine:

- which states retain capacity,
- which trade networks survive,
- how technology changes resilience,
- how food systems adapt,
- and whether emissions remain high or begin declining through substitution and contraction.

## Research Rules for the Historical Timeline

When the fictional master chronology is written:

1. Climate trends may establish pressures, not predetermine exact political outcomes.
2. Named disasters require regional hazard research.
3. Food crises require agricultural and trade research.
4. Migration events require destination as well as origin analysis.
5. State failure requires institutional and fiscal causal chains.
6. Advanced technology must be included in both adaptation and inequality.
7. Recovery between events must be modeled, not ignored.

## Current Canon Boundary

This file establishes no named future events.

It supports only the following provisional structure:

- **2025-2035:** continued warming within a recognizable global order.
- **2035-2050:** adaptation becomes permanent and economically structural.
- **2050-2075:** compound shocks increasingly erode recovery capacity.
- **2075-2100:** severe climate stress and institutional divergence create conditions for the later break in globalism.
