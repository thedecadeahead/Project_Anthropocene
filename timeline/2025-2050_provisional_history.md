# Provisional History: 2025-2050

**Status:** Provisional canon

This document synthesizes current research into a plausible sequence. It does not assign exact wars, governments, casualty totals, or technological breakthroughs unless separately established.

## 2025-2029: Recognition Without Resolution

Climate change is widely acknowledged, but policy remains insufficient to prevent severe warming.

Major trends:

- climate adaptation spending rises;
- insurers increase premiums and retreat from selected high-risk markets;
- governments subsidize rebuilding and assume more private risk;
- artificial intelligence and automation spread through logistics, administration, software, medicine, agriculture, and defense;
- major powers subsidize semiconductors, energy, minerals, and domestic manufacturing;
- and geopolitical competition makes coordinated mitigation more difficult.

Most people still understand disasters as interruptions to normal life.

## 2030-2034: The Adaptation Economy

Climate adaptation becomes a major economic sector.

Likely developments:

- cooling, grid hardening, water recycling, fire defense, flood protection, and protected agriculture expand;
- building codes and mortgage rules begin reflecting long-term climate exposure;
- cities compete for workers and investment by presenting themselves as climate refuges;
- vulnerable households face displacement through cost before physical destruction;
- and regional governments increasingly challenge national policy over land, water, energy, and migration.

Technological development remains rapid, but access becomes more unequal.

## 2035-2039: Managed Retreat Becomes Normal

Repeated disasters make permanent relocation politically unavoidable in several regions.

Likely developments:

- governments formalize buyout and retreat zones;
- public insurers become larger and more fiscally exposed;
- infrastructure investment shifts away from places judged too expensive to defend;
- internal migration increases toward comparatively cooler, wetter, and better-governed regions;
- and destination cities experience housing shortages and political backlash.

The idea that every community will be rebuilt after every disaster begins to disappear.

## 2040-2044: Strategic Resilience

Food, water, energy, chips, medicines, and communications are increasingly treated as security systems.

Likely developments:

- states build reserves and duplicate critical supply chains;
- regional microgrids and hardened data centers expand;
- large farms rely increasingly on automation, advanced forecasting, engineered crops, and controlled irrigation;
- trade remains global but becomes more political and expensive;
- and emergency powers are invoked more frequently during heat, fire, flood, disease, and migration crises.

This is the closing phase of the Late Global Age.

## 2045-2050: The Permanent Emergency Begins

By mid-century, climate response is no longer a separate policy category. It is embedded in budgets, labor law, migration, public health, housing, agriculture, infrastructure, and national security.

Likely developments:

- outdoor work hours shift in heat-stressed regions;
- seasonal cooling and evacuation systems become permanent;
- water allocation becomes a central political issue in multiple basins;
- public debt rises as governments finance protection, relocation, and disaster recovery;
- exposed municipalities struggle to maintain tax bases;
- and regional inequalities deepen between defended zones and areas left to decline.

Globalization still functions, but it is less universal, less trusted, and increasingly organized around strategic blocs.

## Boundary Conditions

By 2050, Project Anthropocene assumes:

- no total global collapse;
- substantial technological progress;
- climate adaptation on an unprecedented scale;
- significant internal migration;
- early financial retreat from high-risk property;
- increased regionalization of trade and infrastructure;
- and the normalization of emergency governance.

The world remains recognizably descended from the present. The old assumptions about permanent growth, universal insurability, and automatic recovery are beginning to fail.
