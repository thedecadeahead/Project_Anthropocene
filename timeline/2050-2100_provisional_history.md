# Provisional History: 2050-2100

**Status:** Provisional canon

This period marks the transition from permanent emergency to systemic fracture.

## 2050-2059: Adaptation Saturation

Governments continue investing in protection, relocation, cooling, water, and food resilience, but the costs rise faster than capacity in many regions.

Likely developments:

- water recycling and desalination protect selected cities while irrigated agriculture contracts elsewhere;
- engineered crops and controlled-environment agriculture reduce some losses but cannot eliminate regional food shocks;
- insurers and lenders withdraw from wider hazard zones;
- climate-receiving cities expand rapidly and unevenly;
- aging populations increase dependence on automation and migrant labor;
- and emergency powers become routine features of government.

The distinction between temporary disaster response and permanent governance largely disappears.

## 2060-2069: Correlated Shocks

Several systems begin failing at the same time rather than in isolation.

Likely developments:

- a major multi-breadbasket harvest shock causes export restrictions and prolonged food-price instability;
- fertilizer, shipping, finance, and fuel disruptions extend agricultural losses into following seasons;
- destination regions struggle to absorb migrants while maintaining housing, water, and services;
- strategic industries are increasingly nationalized or placed under emergency direction;
- and regional blocs harden standards, data systems, payment networks, and supply chains.

The global economy remains active, but poorer and politically isolated regions lose access to critical goods first.

## 2070-2079: The Recovery Gap

The key historical change is that many regions no longer recover fully before the next shock.

Likely developments:

- infrastructure remains damaged or under-maintained between disasters;
- debt and insurance crises constrain rebuilding;
- national governments protect strategic cities, ports, farms, grids, and industrial corridors while reducing peripheral services;
- local utilities, military logistics organizations, corporations, and regional governments assume state functions;
- and recurrent heat emergencies make some cities seasonally dependent on hardened cooling and power systems.

Later historians commonly date the beginning of the Fracture to this decade, though no universally accepted start year exists.

## 2080-2089: The First Global Age Breaks

Global population is near or past its peak, but regional conditions differ sharply.

Likely developments:

- repeated food and water crises trigger new export controls and rationing systems;
- sovereign defaults and municipal fiscal failures expand;
- migration agreements collapse or become coercive labor and settlement systems;
- interstate and civil conflicts damage ports, grids, pipelines, communications, and agricultural regions;
- the global internet fragments into protected blocs and regional networks;
- and leading-edge semiconductor and AI capacity concentrates in a shrinking number of industrial powers.

Long-distance trade continues, but universal market access is no longer assumed.

## 2090-2100: Archipelago Globalism

By the end of the century, the world economy resembles an archipelago of secure systems connected by contested corridors.

Likely developments:

- regional grids operate independently from weakened national networks;
- strategic ports, inland terminals, river systems, and rail corridors become political powers;
- high-value trade persists among defended industrial regions;
- exposed coastal districts and water-stressed interiors undergo large-scale abandonment;
- mature-node electronics, repair, remanufacturing, and salvage become increasingly important;
- and some national governments retain strong authority while others become legal shells over autonomous regions.

## Boundary Conditions

By 2100, Project Anthropocene provisionally assumes:

- approximately 3.2°C global mean warming above 1850-1900, with substantial uncertainty;
- roughly 0.75 meters of global mean sea-level rise relative to approximately 2000;
- severe but uneven climate stress;
- fractured global trade and digital systems;
- persistent advanced technology in protected regions;
- widespread functional retreat of states;
- and no singular worldwide collapse event.

The old world has not ended everywhere. It has stopped being one world.
