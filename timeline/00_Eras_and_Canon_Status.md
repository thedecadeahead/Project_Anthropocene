# Historical Eras and Canon Status

**Status:** Provisional canon framework

Project Anthropocene treats the collapse as a long transition rather than a single event. Era names are analytical labels used by later historians. People living through them would not necessarily use these names.

## The Late Global Age, 2025-2044

The existing international order remains recognizable. Climate damage rises, but global trade, finance, aviation, digital platforms, and nation-states continue functioning.

Defining characteristics:

- accelerating climate adaptation;
- rapid development of artificial intelligence, robotics, biotechnology, and energy systems;
- growing insurance and housing stress;
- increasing industrial policy and strategic competition;
- and repeated regional disasters that are still treated as recoverable.

## The Permanent Emergency, 2045-2074

Emergency measures become normal institutions. Governments increasingly organize housing, food, migration, infrastructure, and labor around recurring climate risk.

Defining characteristics:

- managed retreat and climate receiving zones;
- seasonal heat restrictions;
- strategic grain, water, chip, and energy reserves;
- public assumption of private climate risk;
- widening gaps between protected and exposed regions;
- and growing regionalization of supply chains.

## The Fracture, 2075-2114

Later historians identify this period as the decisive breakdown of the First Global Age.

No single event causes the Fracture. Its defining feature is the loss of recovery capacity between shocks.

Defining characteristics:

- repeated multi-breadbasket crises;
- severe destination stress from migration;
- sovereign debt and insurance failures;
- permanent export controls and bloc economics;
- fragmentation of electrical and digital networks;
- weakened national authority;
- and wars that destroy adaptive capacity.

## The Long Retreat, 2115-2150

Global systems contract into regional blocs, maintenance states, city networks, military districts, and protected industrial corridors.

Defining characteristics:

- functional retreat of many national governments;
- abandonment of exposed coastlines and water-stressed interiors;
- regional grids and intranets replacing national systems;
- population decline in some areas through low fertility, mortality, and out-migration;
- increased dependence on salvage, repair, and standardized technology;
- and the emergence of new political identities.

## Later Eras

The periods after 2150 remain undefined pending regional research. Likely themes include stabilization, reconnection, new state formation, ecological transformation, and the emergence of the principal setting era.

## Canon Rules

- Era boundaries are approximate and may vary by region.
- Named global events remain provisional until supported by regional research.
- The current framework establishes sequence, not exact casualty totals or political borders.
- Every future revision should preserve the principle that collapse occurs unevenly and cumulatively.
