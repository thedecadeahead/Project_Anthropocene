# Everyday Life in 2250

**Status:** Provisional canon

Everyday life in 2250 varies sharply by settlement tier, climate, political system, and access to infrastructure.

The setting should not feel like continuous emergency. People living in 2250 were born into this world. Its routines feel ordinary to them.

## Time and Heat

In hot regions, daily schedules are organized around thermal risk.

Common patterns include:

- early-morning and evening work;
- protected midday rest periods;
- nighttime markets and construction;
- seasonal school calendars;
- heat-status broadcasts;
- and public cooling shelters.

Warm nights are taken seriously because they prevent physical recovery.

## Housing

Housing reflects climate and energy access.

Common features include:

- shaded courtyards;
- reflective surfaces;
- buried or partially buried rooms;
- storm-resistant cores;
- filtered ventilation;
- shared cooling systems;
- water storage;
- modular repairs;
- and rooftop food or power systems.

Old buildings are often heavily modified rather than abandoned.

## Food

Most diets are regional.

Food may come from:

- outdoor farms using adapted crops;
- protected greenhouses;
- automated field systems;
- aquaculture;
- fermentation;
- cultured proteins;
- urban gardens;
- preserved foods;
- and long-distance grain trade.

Wheat bread, fresh fruit, coffee, chocolate, seafood, and other old-world staples vary in availability and may carry strong cultural meaning.

## Work

Common occupations include:

- grid and water technicians;
- agronomists;
- salvage assessors;
- robot maintainers;
- route surveyors;
- couriers and radio operators;
- public-health workers;
- migration coordinators;
- machinists;
- ecosystem rangers;
- and treaty or standards specialists.

Many societies require periods of civic labor for infrastructure, fire defense, flood preparation, food reserves, or emergency response.

## Education

Education emphasizes practical continuity.

Children commonly learn:

- heat and weather safety;
- water discipline;
- local ecology;
- basic repair;
- network literacy;
- first aid;
- civic responsibilities;
- and the history of the Fracture.

Advanced settlements also maintain universities, research institutes, and technical academies.

## Health

Health care ranges from basic clinics to advanced regenerative medicine.

Preventive care focuses heavily on:

- heat exposure;
- respiratory illness;
- water quality;
- nutrition;
- vector-borne disease;
- mental health;
- and occupational injury.

Medical access is one of the clearest distinctions between settlement tiers.

## Family and Community

Households may be biological, chosen, cooperative, contractual, or organized around work and utility membership.

Low fertility and demographic decline make child care, elder care, and migration politically important.

Community survival depends on reciprocal obligations, but these can be supportive or coercive.

## Culture and Leisure

People still create:

- music;
- theater;
- sports;
- games;
- festivals;
- fashion;
- gossip;
- religious rituals;
- and popular media.

Festivals often mark seasonal transitions, water releases, harvests, route openings, first rains, cool nights, or the end of dangerous heat.

Old-world media survives through archives, remakes, local reinterpretations, and incomplete copies.

## Law and Identity

A person may carry several identities:

- settlement resident;
- utility member;
- regional citizen;
- trade-guild member;
- old national descendant;
- and treaty-recognized traveler.

Rights often depend on which authority is being asked.

## Tone

Everyday life should communicate three truths:

1. The world is materially harsher than ours.
2. Advanced systems and ordinary comforts still exist.
3. People do not experience themselves as background characters in an apocalypse.

They are living in the present.
