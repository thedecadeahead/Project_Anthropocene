# Settlement Tiers

**Status:** Provisional canon

Settlement tiers describe access to infrastructure, industry, governance, and regional connections. They are not measures of moral worth, population alone, or cultural sophistication.

A settlement may rank highly in one system and poorly in another. A city with excellent medicine may have weak food security. A rural federation may possess little advanced manufacturing but extraordinary ecological resilience.

## Tier 0 — Abandoned Zone

No stable civil authority or permanent service network.

Typical features:

- ruins, retreat lands, contaminated sites, flood zones, fire corridors, failed irrigation districts, or conflict remnants;
- temporary salvage camps, scientific stations, smugglers, nomads, or seasonal workers;
- intermittent communications;
- no guaranteed water, power, medical care, or legal protection.

Tier 0 does not mean empty. Many supposedly abandoned places contain trapped communities, hidden settlements, autonomous machinery, or contested resources.

## Tier 1 — Local Settlement

A village, neighborhood enclave, mobile community, or isolated town capable of meeting many immediate needs.

Typical features:

- local water and low-voltage power;
- basic medical care;
- repair workshops;
- local food production or dependable seasonal trade;
- radio or intermittent network access;
- limited heavy machinery and manufacturing.

Population may range from a few dozen to several thousand.

## Tier 2 — Networked Town

A settlement integrated into a regional trade, power, or communications network.

Typical features:

- reliable microgrid or regional-grid connection;
- water treatment and organized waste systems;
- schools and technical training;
- clinics or small hospitals;
- machine shops and remanufacturing;
- scheduled freight and passenger service;
- local governance recognized by neighboring authorities.

Tier 2 settlements are the most common stable campaign hubs.

## Tier 3 — Regional City

A major political, industrial, agricultural, or transportation center.

Typical features:

- resilient power and water systems;
- substantial hospitals and higher education;
- regional data centers and AI services;
- mature-node electronics repair or limited fabrication;
- rail, river, road, airship, aviation, or coastal trade links;
- diplomatic institutions and professional security forces;
- capacity to support surrounding settlements.

Tier 3 cities often exercise practical sovereignty even when formally part of a larger state.

## Tier 4 — Maintenance-State Core

A highly integrated urban-industrial region capable of reproducing complex systems.

Typical features:

- advanced manufacturing;
- calibration and standards institutions;
- major research centers;
- hardened grids and protected communications;
- pharmaceutical, aerospace, semiconductor, reactor, or biotechnology capacity;
- regional or intercontinental influence;
- formal citizenship, labor, and resource systems.

Only a limited number of Tier 4 regions exist. They depend on trade and cannot manufacture every strategic input independently.

## Tier 5 — Global Node

A rare center connecting multiple regional blocs through technology, finance, diplomacy, transport, or communications.

Typical features:

- continuous or near-continuous intercontinental links;
- leading-edge research and computing;
- high-value manufacturing and trade finance;
- neutral institutions or great-power protection;
- access to orbital, oceanic, and long-distance infrastructure.

A Tier 5 node is not necessarily the largest city. Its importance comes from connectivity and irreplaceable capabilities.

## Mixed-Tier Settlements

Most large settlements are internally uneven.

A protected core may operate at Tier 4 while outer districts function at Tier 1 or Tier 2. Informal communities may exist beside advanced hospitals, data centers, and transit systems without equal access.

## Campaign Use

Settlement tier helps determine:

- available equipment and services;
- legal authority;
- communications delay;
- likely occupations;
- repair capability;
- travel connections;
- and the consequences of infrastructure failure.

A settlement's tier can rise or fall through campaign events.
