# Settlement Anchors

**Status:** Provisional canon

These locations are initial campaign anchors, not final city sourcebooks.

## Chicago Continuity

A vast inhabited metropolitan ruin and one of the region's most advanced nodes.

Campaign functions:

- diplomacy,
- industrial contracts,
- espionage,
- rail travel,
- social inequality,
- and access to advanced technology.

The city contains fully maintained districts, repurposed towers, flooded or abandoned zones, automated infrastructure, informal settlements, and sealed old-world facilities.

## St. Louis Confluence

A strategic settlement complex where the Missouri and Mississippi systems meet.

Campaign functions:

- convoy formation,
- river politics,
- salvage markets,
- flood engineering,
- and negotiation between major regional powers.

The old metropolitan area is divided among fortified high ground, working river terminals, reclaimed industrial districts, floodplain settlements, and ruins repeatedly altered by the rivers.

## Twin Cities Headwater League

A northern river and rail center controlling key upper Mississippi infrastructure.

Campaign functions:

- water-release disputes,
- cold-region agriculture,
- technical education,
- archive work,
- and political tension between urban and rural districts.

## Detroit–Windsor Works

A binational industrial zone that survives through manufacturing, vehicle systems, lake trade, and cross-border institutions.

Campaign functions:

- machine production,
- labor politics,
- smuggling,
- border law,
- robotics,
- and industrial sabotage.

## Duluth Superior Gate

The western gateway to Lake Superior and northern resource routes.

Campaign functions:

- mining trade,
- lake convoys,
- wilderness expeditions,
- Indigenous diplomacy,
- and competition over strategic materials.

## Cincinnati Riverworks

A fortified Ohio River industrial and medical center.

Campaign functions:

- medical technology,
- river manufacturing,
- legal arbitration,
- quarantine operations,
- and contested lock infrastructure.

## Cairo Junction

A difficult but strategically critical settlement near the meeting of the Ohio and Mississippi.

Campaign functions:

- customs enforcement,
- flood survival,
- convoy transfer,
- black markets,
- and disputes between upriver and downriver powers.

The settlement may shift physically over time as channels and defenses change.

## Reservoir Towns

Smaller settlements clustered near dams, pumping stations, treatment plants, and hydropower facilities.

Campaign functions:

- local mysteries,
- infrastructure repair,
- water politics,
- isolated communities,
- and conflicts over technical succession.

## Corridor Stations

Fortified rail, road, fiber, or river service settlements.

Campaign functions:

- resupply,
- rumors,
- customs checks,
- short adventures,
- and visible transitions between political jurisdictions.

## Ruin Settlements

Communities living inside partially abandoned cities, suburbs, factories, malls, campuses, or logistics centers.

They may survive through:

- salvage,
- urban farming,
- local manufacturing,
- data recovery,
- convoy services,
- or control of one surviving piece of infrastructure.

## Development Rule

Each future settlement sourcebook should define:

- what keeps the settlement alive,
- what it cannot produce,
- which infrastructure it depends upon,
- who is excluded from its protections,
- what old-world geography still shapes it,
- and what immediate problems create playable stories.
