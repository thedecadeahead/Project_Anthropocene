# Successor Powers

**Status:** Provisional canon

The Great Lakes–Mississippi region is governed by overlapping authorities rather than a clean map of nation-states.

## The Lake Compact

A federation of major Great Lakes ports, water authorities, industrial cities, and basin governments.

### Strengths

- freshwater access,
- shipyards and heavy industry,
- regional grids,
- advanced medicine,
- technical education,
- and strong lake transport.

### Weaknesses

- internal rivalry between ports,
- expensive shoreline defense,
- dependence on imported materials,
- and conflict over water exports beyond the basin.

### Political Character

Technocratic and treaty-driven. Membership is often tied to compliance with basin standards rather than cultural identity.

## The River Union

A loose network of Mississippi and Ohio river cities, lock authorities, agricultural districts, and convoy guilds.

### Strengths

- control of inland freight,
- agricultural exchange,
- river engineering,
- and political reach across many smaller settlements.

### Weaknesses

- fragile unity,
- dependence on navigable water levels,
- piracy and toll disputes,
- and conflict between upstream and downstream interests.

### Political Character

Commercial, federal, and intensely procedural. Its laws are designed around cargo, passage rights, dredging obligations, and flood liability.

## The Missouri Reach

A chain of fortified reservoir cities, agricultural cooperatives, military engineering districts, and transport towns along the Missouri corridor.

### Strengths

- reservoir control,
- hydropower,
- western trade access,
- and hardened logistics culture.

### Weaknesses

- chronic water disputes,
- exposure to drought and extreme flooding,
- long vulnerable transport distances,
- and rivalry between civilian and military authorities.

### Political Character

Pragmatic, security-focused, and suspicious of outside control.

## The Chicago Continuity

A dense successor metropolis built around surviving rail, lake, data, finance, and manufacturing systems in the Chicago region.

### Strengths

- population,
- rail convergence,
- advanced industry,
- communications,
- and institutional continuity.

### Weaknesses

- enormous maintenance burden,
- food and water politics,
- internal inequality,
- and dependence on negotiated access through surrounding territories.

### Political Character

A layered civic state composed of municipal districts, infrastructure boards, unions, commercial houses, and security institutions.

## The Superior Cooperative Zone

A northern federation of mining towns, Indigenous governments, port communities, forest settlements, and energy sites around Lake Superior.

### Strengths

- minerals,
- forests,
- cool climate,
- hydropower,
- and defensible terrain.

### Weaknesses

- sparse population,
- difficult winters,
- distance from dense markets,
- and pressure from outside powers seeking resources.

### Political Character

Decentralized and treaty-based, with strong local autonomy.

## The Delta Remnants

Communities, port enclaves, floating settlements, salvage fleets, and flood-control authorities in the lower Mississippi transition zone.

### Strengths

- maritime access,
- salvage,
- fisheries,
- and control of routes between inland and ocean trade.

### Weaknesses

- severe flood and storm exposure,
- unstable land,
- salinity,
- and fragmented authority.

### Political Character

Mobile, adaptive, and commercially opportunistic.

## Independent Actors

Important non-state powers include:

- convoy guilds,
- rail cooperatives,
- water boards,
- salvage syndicates,
- Indigenous nations,
- agricultural federations,
- technical orders,
- religious communities,
- private security houses,
- and archive institutions.

## Canon Boundary

These powers define the first political sketch. Exact borders, capitals, leaders, constitutions, and military structures remain open for later batches.
