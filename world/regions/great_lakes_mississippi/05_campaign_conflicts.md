# Campaign Conflicts

**Status:** Provisional canon

Project Anthropocene campaigns should be able to generate tension without defaulting to endless raider violence.

## Water Release Dispute

An upstream authority reduces reservoir releases during drought, threatening downstream cities, farms, navigation, and power generation.

Possible campaign roles:

- technical investigators,
- negotiators,
- convoy escorts,
- saboteurs,
- or representatives of affected settlements.

## Broken Lock Crisis

A critical lock fails during the trade season, trapping cargo and causing food, fuel, and medicine shortages.

The problem may involve:

- scarce replacement components,
- competing repair plans,
- sabotage allegations,
- labor disputes,
- or a political attempt to redirect trade.

## The Refuge Settlement

A regional power proposes a new settlement for displaced people in land already used by rural communities, Indigenous nations, or ecological restoration projects.

The conflict concerns membership, land, water, labor, and who bears the cost of adaptation.

## Seed Treaty Theft

A shipment of heat-adapted seed stock or biological material disappears in transit.

Possible causes include:

- commercial espionage,
- desperate farmers,
- political separatists,
- contamination fears,
- or a faction seeking control over future harvests.

## Grid Island Failure

A regional electrical island loses a critical transformer, control system, or power-electronics component.

The party may need to locate a replacement in an abandoned industrial zone, negotiate access to a rival grid, or choose which districts receive limited power.

## Archive Dispute

Recovered records appear to alter the ownership of water rights, land, infrastructure, or technical intellectual property.

The campaign centers on authenticity, historical justice, political legitimacy, and whether old-world law should govern the present.

## Floodplain Secession

A flood-control authority intentionally sacrifices peripheral settlements to protect a major city.

Survivors form an armed political movement and threaten levees, shipping, or water infrastructure.

## Industrial Defection

A group of engineers, doctors, chip specialists, or reactor technicians attempts to leave one maintenance state for another.

Their employers consider them strategic assets rather than ordinary citizens.

## The Dead Corridor

A once-protected rail, road, or fiber route stops communicating.

Potential causes include:

- bridge failure,
- flood damage,
- epidemic quarantine,
- autonomous security systems,
- local rebellion,
- or simple institutional abandonment.

## Lake Treaty Violation

A power begins exporting basin water, polluting a shared watershed, diverting a river, or deploying prohibited military systems on the lakes.

The dispute can escalate through sanctions, covert operations, blockade, or arbitration before open war.

## Salvage Rights

Several factions claim the same abandoned facility:

- descendants of its workers,
- the authority controlling the land,
- the convoy that rediscovered it,
- an archive holding old ownership records,
- and a community that needs its equipment to survive.

## Campaign Design Principle

Every regional conflict should identify:

1. the material system at stake;
2. the groups that depend on it;
3. the historical grievance shaping the dispute;
4. the technical facts that constrain possible solutions;
5. the cost of compromise;
6. and the consequences if no one acts.

Violence may occur, but it should emerge from political and material stakes rather than exist as environmental decoration.
