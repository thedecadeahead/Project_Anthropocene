# Climate and Geography

**Status:** Provisional canon

## Regional Overview

The Great Lakes–Mississippi system remains one of North America's largest connected freshwater and transport landscapes in 2250.

It is more habitable than many hotter and drier regions, but it is not a climate refuge in the simplistic sense. The region faces:

- hotter summers and more dangerous humid heat,
- intense precipitation and river flooding,
- periods of drought and low-water navigation,
- severe storms,
- wildfire smoke from distant and regional fires,
- changing lake levels and shorelines,
- invasive species and ecological turnover,
- and repeated infrastructure damage.

Its relative advantage comes from freshwater, soils, transport geography, industrial inheritance, and a climate that remains manageable with adaptation.

## The Great Lakes

The lakes function as:

- freshwater reservoirs,
- inland seas,
- transportation corridors,
- climate moderators,
- fisheries,
- political borders,
- and strategic military space.

Lake levels fluctuate over years and decades. Shore communities must manage erosion, flooding, ice changes, storms, and altered wetlands.

The lakes are not one political unit. Access is governed through competing port authorities, basin treaties, municipal leagues, Indigenous governments, industrial states, and military agreements.

## The Mississippi System

The Mississippi and its tributaries are the main connective tissue of the interior.

Their uses include:

- bulk freight,
- passenger movement,
- irrigation,
- drinking water,
- hydropower,
- flood control,
- sewage transport,
- fisheries,
- and territorial boundaries.

The river is more engineered than ever, but control is incomplete. Abandoned levees, shifting channels, sediment loss, stronger floods, low-water years, and neglected tributaries continually alter the navigable landscape.

## The Missouri River

The Missouri is a difficult but strategically essential western route.

It links water-stressed plains and mountain corridors to the Mississippi system. Its dams, reservoirs, agricultural withdrawals, and navigation channels are politically contested.

Control of its flow affects:

- downstream shipping,
- irrigation,
- municipal water,
- hydropower,
- flood exposure,
- and settlement viability.

## The Ohio Corridor

The Ohio remains one of the most valuable east–west industrial and river corridors.

Former manufacturing cities provide:

- salvage,
- machine shops,
- rail connections,
- lock and dam infrastructure,
- skilled populations,
- and defensible urban terrain.

## Agricultural Zones

Agriculture persists but changes substantially.

The region produces combinations of:

- heat-adapted grains,
- soy and legumes,
- perennial crops,
- greenhouse produce,
- controlled-environment food,
- livestock in cooler subregions,
- aquaculture,
- and industrial fermentation products.

Historic monocultures decline where heat, rainfall volatility, soil degradation, and input scarcity make them too risky.

## Ecological Change

Abandoned farmland, suburbs, industrial corridors, and floodplains create large rewilding zones.

These landscapes may include:

- expanding forests,
- restored and unmanaged wetlands,
- large herbivores,
- predators,
- feral domestic species,
- invasive plants and fish,
- and engineered or assisted-migration species.

Rewilded does not mean untouched. Many ecosystems are novel combinations shaped by climate, contamination, migration, and human management.

## Regional Hazard Pattern

### Summer

Humid heat, severe storms, algae blooms, crop stress, and high cooling demand.

### Autumn

Harvest logistics, wildfire smoke, river-level uncertainty, and major trade movement.

### Winter

Shorter and less reliable ice seasons, freeze-thaw damage, storms, and heating stress.

### Spring

Flooding, planting uncertainty, infrastructure washouts, and waterborne contamination.

## Playable Geography Principle

The region should not be divided into safe and unsafe zones.

Instead, every corridor presents a different balance of:

- water,
- infrastructure,
- climate exposure,
- political authority,
- industrial capacity,
- and ecological risk.
