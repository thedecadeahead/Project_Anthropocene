# Routes and Trade

**Status:** Provisional canon

## Regional Principle

Movement is easiest where infrastructure, authority, and geography overlap.

A road may physically exist but be unusable because bridges failed, fuel is unavailable, toll rights are disputed, or no authority guarantees safe passage.

## The Lake Routes

Large lake vessels move bulk cargo between defended ports.

Common cargo includes:

- grain,
- metal,
- timber,
- machine parts,
- fertilizer,
- medicines,
- and passengers.

Navigation depends on dredging, port maintenance, weather forecasting, and treaty access.

## The Mississippi Spine

The Mississippi is the region's primary north–south freight artery.

Movement relies on:

- locks,
- dredged channels,
- protected terminals,
- convoy escorts,
- river pilots,
- and water-level forecasts.

Low-water years can be as disruptive as floods.

## The Missouri Reach

The Missouri corridor links the interior to western settlements, reservoirs, mines, and dryland agriculture.

Travel is slower and more political than on the upper Mississippi. Reservoir releases, damaged dams, shifting channels, and armed toll stations can alter routes quickly.

## The Ohio Industrial Route

The Ohio connects manufacturing regions, coal and mineral districts, river cities, and eastern trade gateways.

It carries a disproportionate share of:

- machine tools,
- industrial chemicals,
- rail components,
- medical supplies,
- and skilled workers.

## Rail Corridors

Rail survives where regional powers can standardize parts, maintain track, protect bridges, and coordinate schedules.

Rail is the preferred movement system for high-volume inland freight where functioning lines exist.

Major rail junctions become heavily defended political centers.

## Protected Roads

Road travel remains common near major settlements, but long-distance highway networks are fragmented.

Protected road corridors may use:

- scheduled convoys,
- autonomous freight vehicles,
- fortified service stations,
- toll checkpoints,
- and emergency shelters.

Outside maintained corridors, old highways become local roads, flood channels, farms, ruins, or forest routes.

## Air Travel

Aviation survives for high-value passengers, medicine, reconnaissance, and urgent freight.

It is expensive and concentrated around regional capitals, military facilities, and industrial powers.

Short-range electric or hybrid aircraft may connect secure hubs, but aviation is not the default means of ordinary travel.

## Communications Corridors

Fiber often follows the same rail, road, pipeline, and utility corridors as physical trade.

Long-distance communication is strongest between major nodes and unreliable in peripheral zones.

Riverboats, trains, and convoy vehicles may physically carry encrypted archives and delayed messages when networks are interrupted.

## Strategic Trade Goods

The most politically important goods include:

- grain and preserved food,
- clean water equipment,
- seed stock,
- fertilizer and nutrients,
- electrical components,
- semiconductors,
- medicines and biological materials,
- machine tools,
- batteries,
- industrial chemicals,
- and technical documentation.

## Trade Law

Trade agreements commonly specify:

- transit rights,
- salvage ownership,
- repair obligations,
- convoy protection,
- water releases,
- quarantine procedures,
- data exchange,
- and arbitration.

Breaking a maintenance or navigation obligation can be treated as seriously as a military attack.
