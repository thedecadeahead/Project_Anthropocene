# Technology Access Tiers

**Status:** Provisional canon

Technology in 2250 is advanced, uneven, inherited, locally reinvented, and constrained by maintenance capacity.

A society's technology tier describes what it can reliably reproduce and sustain—not merely what artifacts it possesses.

## Tier A — Salvaged Technology

Communities operate inherited machines and recovered equipment but cannot reproduce most critical components.

Typical capabilities:

- basic solar and battery systems;
- repaired vehicles;
- radio communications;
- simple drones;
- legacy computers;
- mechanical workshops;
- cannibalized medical and agricultural equipment.

Failure of a rare component can permanently remove a capability.

## Tier B — Durable Regional Technology

Communities can maintain and reproduce rugged systems based on mature industrial processes.

Typical capabilities:

- standardized electric vehicles;
- mature-node electronics;
- local servers and small AI models;
- modular robots;
- machine tools and additive manufacturing;
- reliable water treatment;
- common pharmaceuticals;
- protected agriculture and automated farms.

This is the technological baseline of many stable towns and regional states.

## Tier C — Advanced Industrial Technology

Regions can reproduce complex machines and maintain specialized supply chains.

Typical capabilities:

- high-performance computing;
- advanced robotics;
- modern aviation;
- sophisticated biotechnology;
- complex medical devices;
- grid-scale storage;
- advanced reactors;
- precision manufacturing;
- and limited semiconductor fabrication.

Tier C requires technical education, standards, metrology, chemical processing, and reliable trade.

## Tier D — Frontier Technology

A small number of powers retain or exceed late-21st-century frontier capability.

Possible capabilities:

- leading-edge semiconductor fabrication;
- frontier AI training;
- advanced gene therapies;
- autonomous industrial systems;
- orbital infrastructure;
- high-performance materials;
- fusion energy if established by later canon;
- and large-scale climate engineering research.

Tier D technology should remain rare, politically controlled, and dependent on broad industrial networks.

## Access Versus Capability

A settlement may host technology it cannot reproduce.

Examples:

- a Tier B city operating a century-old Tier C medical scanner;
- a Tier A community protected by an inherited autonomous defense system;
- a Tier C state dependent on a foreign Tier D supplier for processors;
- a rural cooperative using advanced seed lines but lacking the laboratories that created them.

## Design Principles

- Older technology is not automatically primitive.
- Simpler systems may be more strategically valuable because they are reproducible.
- Consumer convenience declines faster than essential industrial capability.
- Repairability and standardization matter more than novelty.
- AI remains common, but frontier AI remains concentrated.
- Technology varies by sector within the same society.

## Campaign Use

Technology tiers determine:

- what equipment can be purchased;
- what failures can be repaired locally;
- how valuable salvage is;
- which specialists are politically important;
- and what trade dependencies drive conflict or diplomacy.
